# Part II, Chapter 5: The Ethics of Eating Murloc

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

I will not pretend the subject is comfortable. I will address it plainly and then we can all move on.

There was a winter — third year of the second blight, or perhaps the fourth, the years have a tendency to blur when one is constantly rebuilding walls — in which the supply wagons did not come and the lake was frozen solid except in the middle where the murlocs live. I will not go into further detail. I ate murloc. So did Aldric Thatcher, who is now a perfectly respectable mill operator in Southshore, and he does not appreciate being reminded of it at the harvest fair, so I would ask you to keep that between us.

The primary obstacle to murloc as a food source is the smell. I will not soften this for you: raw murloc smells like low tide attended by a dispute. It is a wet, argumentative smell. It gets into the clothes, the hair, the general sense of self. However — and I say this with the weary authority of someone who has done the necessary research — the smell is entirely soluble in salt. You want more salt than you think. Then more than that. Then one final handful, because you are dealing with a creature whose entire communicative vocabulary is "mrglmrgl," and the universe has arranged for it to taste accordingly without further intervention.

Here is the method that works. Scale and gut the murloc immediately — do not wait, I cannot stress this sufficiently — and soak the portions in brine for no less than four hours. Eight is better. A full day is ideal, if the situation allows. Then bring to a rolling boil in fresh water with whatever aromatics you have: ginger is best, and worth going out of your way for; bay leaf helps; dried onion does something useful. Boil for forty minutes. Not thirty. Forty. Skim what rises. Boil another ten for good measure. The texture will never be mistaken for pheasant. But it will be edible. It will, in fact, in the right circumstances — with enough salt, a reasonable fire, and a certain honest lowering of expectations — be almost fine.

The ethics of the thing are as follows: I thought about it. I was hungry. The murlocs were there, and aggressive besides. I made my decision and I stand by it, though I would not frame it as a triumph of virtue. There are those who argue the murloc is a sapient creature possessed of dignity and community and perhaps something approaching language. I have heard this argument. I have also heard them scream at me for forty solid seconds while I tried to collect water from a stream I had every right to use. I will not be lectured on murloc dignity by someone who has not been screamed at by a murloc. That is my final word on the ethics.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The murloc presents a rare and clarifying case study in the ethics of consumption, and I find myself returning to it with something approaching gratitude.

The soul of the murloc is, and I say this with professional precision, a mess. Not a complex mess — it is not the elegant disorganization of, say, a troll's spiritual topology — it is simply chaotic in the manner of something that never developed the organizational habit. Murloc souls resist binding the way wet sand resists architecture: not through opposition, but through a kind of fundamental formlessness. The Cult has attempted to catalogue them twice. Both times, the catalogue became incoherent by the third entry. We have abandoned the project. This is relevant.

The practical consequence is this: when you consume a murloc, you create no resource conflict. A peasant who eats a wolf removes something the Scourge might otherwise raise in service of the Lich King. A peasant who fells a grove deprives possible banshees of refuge. A peasant who eats a murloc has consumed something that was, spiritually speaking, already inaccessible to us. The murloc passes through life and exits it in a state we cannot use. Its soul disperses like smoke in a crosswind, and we have never been able to determine where it goes. Perhaps nowhere. Perhaps a different somewhere entirely. The Cult does not speculate; we catalogue, and when we cannot catalogue, we acknowledge the limitation and move on.

This conclusion is, I think, genuinely elegant. Eating murloc is one of the few dietary decisions that carries no downstream spiritual complications whatsoever. In a world where nearly every act of consumption has implications for the undead economy, the murloc stands alone as purely, unambiguously available. I would not recommend it on the basis of flavour. But ethically? I cannot fault it. It is, in its chaotic, disorganized way, the cleanest meal one can eat. I find this almost beautiful.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

We have watched murlocs since before the word "murloc" existed, which was also before the word "watched," so perhaps we mean something slightly different by both.

They make one sound, and it changes. This is not the same as making many sounds. We have spent considerable time — considerable in the way that light through water is considerable, which is not a unit you can measure — trying to understand whether the variations in mrgl constitute language, or whether language is too small a concept for what mrgl is. We have not resolved this. We suspect resolution is the wrong tool.

They are afraid, and they are not afraid. They scream and then they go back to the water and the water is the same water and they are the same murlocs and the screaming is also still present in some form, vibrating in something. They do not seem to distinguish between danger that passed and danger that is. This would concern us, except that we are not entirely sure the distinction is real.

We do not eat. This is not a moral position. We are made of light and the memory of trees and something that accumulates in still pools at the edges of things. We are not structured to consume. But we understand the argument the Peasant is making: you do what you must, in the body you have, in the winter that arrives. We do not find fault with this.

We only mention: the mrgl goes on for longer than you think. There is structure in it, if you slow down enough to be inside it rather than next to it. We are not saying the murloc should not be eaten. We are saying: you could also sit by the water and listen, and something would happen that we cannot name, and you would be different afterward in a way that does not require translation.

You do not have to. The salt is probably fine.
