# Bonus Chapter: The Tefal Clean It Textile Cleaner, 750 Watt

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

At last. A tool that understands the basic principle that cloth, once soiled, ought to become less soiled rather than simply accepted as having entered a new permanent condition. I am in favour of this immediately. Upholstery is one of those domestic categories people neglect because they imagine that if a chair remains shaped like a chair, then no maintenance issue exists. This is false. A chair may continue to be recognisably a chair while also harbouring enough dust, grease, dog, soup, and old disappointment in its fabric to qualify as a minor ecosystem. Soft furnishings hold the history of a household in a way I find frankly accusatory.

A dedicated textile cleaner is therefore a civilised thing. Seven hundred and fifty watts is a reassuring number: not absurdly theatrical, not insultingly timid. Enough force, one hopes, to draw dirt upward and outward from fibres without turning the whole enterprise into the indoor equivalent of flood damage. The important thing with any machine of this sort is not enthusiasm but restraint. You do not drench the armchair as though trying to baptise it into a better life. You test an inconspicuous patch first. You work methodically. You allow proper drying time. And for heaven's sake, if the collection chamber shows you what has come out of the upholstery, treat that revelation as a call to continued maintenance rather than a reason to sit down and have a crisis.

I will add one caution. The existence of a machine does not absolve the household of ordinary standards. Mud on boots should still remain outdoors. Gravy should still be conveyed to the mouth with some dignity. Children should still be told, repeatedly, that the sofa is not a siege engine. A textile cleaner is not permission to live like animals. It is a remedy for the fact that many people do.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Morgum likes this thing. Morgum likes it because it is honest. The machine says what it does in the name: clean it. Very clear. No mystery. No lordly poetry. No false promises about lifestyle. Just command. Cloth is dirty? Clean it. This is good communication between tool and worker.

Morgum has opinions about cloth. Cloth is weak. Cloth remembers everything. Sit on it one time after hunt and now the chair smells like outside forever. Spill stew once and the bench keeps the story long after everyone is tired of hearing it. A machine that pushes water in, grabs filth out, and leaves the cushion less shameful is useful in exactly the way useful things ought to be useful. Also the long nose on it is good. Morgum respects any tool with a nose, because nose means the tool is trying to reach somewhere difficult without making you crawl fully under furniture like a desperate rat.

Seven hundred and fifty watts sounds strong. Morgum does not know what watt is exactly. Morgum assumes watt is city-person word for how angry the machine can become before it starts helping. Seven hundred and fifty of them seems enough. Not too many. If machine too strong, maybe it eats blanket. If machine too weak, it just licks the stain and leaves. Morgum wants middle strength. Good pull. Serious slurp. If friend receives this as gift, friend should know this means respect. You do not give textile-cleaning beast to someone you dislike. You give it because you want their chair to survive.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

Textiles are troublesome. Stone can be brushed. Bone can be polished. Metal can be burnished back to a truthful surface with patience and correct compounds. Cloth, by contrast, absorbs. It receives the world too willingly. It takes in dust, oils, smoke, skin, damp, candle soot, corridor air, the residue of years, and then has the discourtesy to continue looking decorative while doing so. For this reason, I have long regarded upholstered furnishings as aesthetically appealing but spiritually untrustworthy.

And yet I find myself admiring the device under discussion. Extraction cleaning is, at heart, a ritual of reversal: what entered the fibres is compelled to leave them. The machine applies water in measured quantity, agitates the contamination loose, and removes it before the textile has time to make peace with its condition. This is good practice. The wattage is, as the Peasant notes, respectable. One need not make a performance of power when addressing decay. One requires only sufficient authority. There is something deeply satisfying in the visible collection of what was formerly hidden. It gives form to neglect. It says: here is the exact volume of inattention your armchair has accumulated.

I would not, however, use such a machine carelessly on ceremonial drapery, ancient hangings, stitched funerary cloth, or anything whose stains have become historically significant. Preservation is not identical with cleanliness. Some marks are damage. Some are record. A wise household distinguishes between the two before introducing moisture and suction. For ordinary domestic upholstery, though — for the chair in which a living man has eaten, slept, worried, perspired, and shed the season into the weave — yes. Clean it. The machine's blunt imperative is correct.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The cloth sat beneath the body and learned the body's weather. Heat pressed into it. Dust settled into it. Rain arrived as steam from drying boots. Afternoon light laid itself over the arm of the chair in the same place every day until the fabric remembered the sun better than the room did. Nothing touched the textile only once. This is what cloth is for. It keeps the shape of contact after contact has gone elsewhere.

Then a new creature arrives: not hand, not rain, not wind. It speaks in a single purpose. Water descends into the weave. A pulling follows. What was hidden between threads begins to travel. The old dust leaves not as dust but as dark water, which is another way of saying that the chair tells the truth only when persuaded. The machine does not erase the sitting. It removes what the sitting shed. The difference matters, though only to those who believe memories and residues are the same thing.

Seven hundred and fifty is a number the machine carries the way thunder carries distance: as a declaration that something will happen soon. The room hears it. The cloth hears it. The stain hears it last. Then the fibres loosen their grip on the small remains of living, and the water carries them away in a temporary river through a plastic chamber. The chair becomes lighter. Not innocent. Not new. Simply less burdened by what it no longer needs to remember.

The roots clean differently. Rain passes down, soil takes what falls, wind dries, sun bleaches, moss covers, time decides what remains. This machine is a faster season for indoor things. The creatures have built a weather system with a handle and a tank and a promise. It is a strange promise. It is not an empty one.
