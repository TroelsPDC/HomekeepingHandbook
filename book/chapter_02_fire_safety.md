# Part IV, Chapter 6: Fire Safety

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

Fire is not an enemy. Fire is a covenant you make with your home, and like all covenants, it requires maintenance or it will eventually destroy everything.

The hearth must be swept of ash no fewer than once per week. An ash-choked hearth draws poorly, smokes into the room rather than up the chimney, and one particularly cold evening will remind you of this in a way you will not soon forget. The chimney must be inspected each autumn, before the burning season begins in earnest. Do not inspect it from below. You will regret this. Use a rod.

Keep water within reach of the hearth at all times. Not so close as to steam, but close enough to pour. A small fire extinguished in the first moments of foolishness costs you a bucket of water and an evening of embarrassment. The same fire extinguished three minutes later costs you everything you own, possibly including the neighbour's barn.

I have witnessed both outcomes. Neither is pleasant, but one is considerably more expensive, and the embarrassment does not diminish with distance.


If anyone asks whether this maintenance plan can be carried by an African or European swallow, hand them a ladder and let them prove it.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The Scourge maintains its structures in perpetual preparation for fire: we build in stone, we light in controlled increments, and we regard combustible arrangements of wood and thatch with the mild concern of those who have already considered what happens when they burn.

Fire is primarily a threat to the living, who have arranged their dwellings to be warm rather than permanent. This is a meaningful choice. We do not judge it. We simply note that stone lasts and wood does not, and that the preference for impermanence is a cultural trait rather than a structural necessity.

If you insist upon maintaining a structure that can burn, the following is advised: never leave the flame unwatched, do not store pitch near the hearthside, and accept — philosophically — what you have chosen. The Lich King does not mourn burned towers. The Lich King notes them.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

Fire remembers the sun.

Wood that burns has always wanted to. You see destruction. We see reunion. The smoke is the tree leaving through the only door you left open. The ash is the tree having arrived where it was going.

We do not keep fire inside, because we do not keep inside inside. The light is everywhere, if you do not build walls to block it.

We are sorry your roof burned. The roof was briefly free. This is not a comfortable observation and we understand if it does not help.
