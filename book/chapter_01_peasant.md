# Chapter One: The Peasant's Account

---

They told us to build a tower. Up on the hill, past the broken fence, close enough to the battle that the shouting comes and goes with the wind. "Ready to work!" they said.

Right. Yes. Ready forever, apparently.

I grabbed my hammer. Grabbed my lumber. Grabbed rope, because rope solves half of life's arguments. The sun was already up, which meant we were late, which meant someone important would clear his throat at us. They always do.

The hill was steep. My boots slipped twice. Good boots, mostly. Tired boots. Like the rest of us.

Geralt was with me. Good lad. Quiet. Didn't talk much, which was fine, because I didn't need poetry, I needed someone to hold the far end of a beam. He was very good at that.

We started on the foundation. Dig, set, fill, pack. Dig, set, fill, pack. You get into a rhythm after a while. A nice bouncy misery to it.

"More work?" the quartermaster shouted from below, like he'd invented the concept.

"Yes, m'lord," I shouted back. Respectful-like. Very respectful.

The battle was close. Closer than I liked. Steel clashing, men shouting, and once a noise I decided not to identify for my own peace of mind. But you don't stop. If builders stopped every time war got noisy, we'd all still be living in mud.

We laid the first floor by noon. I was hungry enough to chew my glove. Geralt handed me half a roll. Good lad.

By mid-afternoon the walls were going up. The fighting had moved a little south, which was a relief. Still noisy, but distant-noisy, which is a kinder sort of noise.

That's when the arrow came in to improve the day.

I didn't see it. I heard it. Then I heard Geralt sit down — not fall, just sit, like his legs had politely resigned. He looked at the arrow in his shoulder like it had turned up at the wrong address.

I got him behind the wall. Packed the wound. Tore a strip off my shirt. "You're going to be fine," I said, because that's what you say when you've got no better medicine than confidence and old cloth.

He nodded like he believed me, which was kind of him.

I finished the tower alone. Took longer. Didn't do the top bit as neat as I'd have liked. If anyone complains, they're welcome to dodge arrows and do trim work themselves.

When they came to get Geralt, the captain looked up at the tower and nodded. "Good work," he said.

"Off I go then," I told nobody in particular, and hauled the last plank into place.

Work, work.

That's all it ever is.
