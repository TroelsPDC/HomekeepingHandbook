# Part III, Chapter 4: Proper Banner Placement

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

A banner is a statement. I want you to understand that before we discuss placement, because placement follows from meaning, and meaning is the thing most men get wrong when they pick up a length of cloth and a pole and start deciding things. Your banner says: I am here, I am of this house, I serve this lord, this is the place we inhabit. It does not say: I am very important. It does not say: Look how high up I have managed to put a thing. And yet. And yet.

The correct placement for a household banner is above the main door, at a height that can be reached with a standard ladder and maintained without the involvement of a second person and a rope. This is not a limitation; this is a specification. A banner at correct height can be cleaned when it is soiled, repaired when it is torn, lowered when the lord commands a sign of mourning, and generally tended to as the statement of loyalty it is meant to be. A banner at incorrect height can do none of these things. A banner at incorrect height is a banner that has been installed in defiance of physics and good sense, and in my experience, a man who installs his banner too high is a man who has confused visibility with significance. He believes the banner should be seen at great distance. It should not, primarily, be seen at great distance. It should primarily be there. There is a difference, and the difference is everything.

I have seen men put banners on the peak of their roofs. I have seen this with my own eyes and I am still not entirely reconciled to it. The peak of the roof is where rain drains away from and where birds stand to look at things. It is not a symbolic location in any political sense I have encountered. A banner on the peak of a roof suggests a man who has run out of wall and refused to accept the conclusion. Furthermore, the structural load of a banner pole sunk into roofing material is not trivial, particularly in wind — I have patched the damage, three separate households, all three put the banner there because their neighbour had put his banner very high and they wished to be higher. This is not how banners work. This is not how roofs work. The roof will lose this argument and you will pay for it in timber.

The banner should be of a size appropriate to the structure. I have a formula, arrived at through observation rather than calculation: if your banner, when seen from the road, causes a passerby to remark on the banner before remarking on the building, it is too large. The building is the statement. The banner is the footnote. A very loyal footnote, I grant you, and I mean no disrespect to the lord's device, but a footnote nonetheless. The cloth should be clean. The colours should be true. The device should be recognizable at distance and correct — if you are not certain of the correct device, consult the lord's household before putting anything up. I say this because I have seen a man fly the wrong banner for eleven years, and the day it was pointed out to him was a very difficult day for everyone present.

The pole should be attached with iron brackets, not wooden pegs. Wood rots. The angle should be slightly outward — no more than fifteen degrees from the wall — so that in still air the banner falls free and is visible, and in wind the angle gives room to move without fouling on the stonework. This is an afternoon's work. Done properly, it should not need revisiting for several years. I am always faintly amazed that it is not more often done correctly the first time.

> *Acolyte annotation: The footnote framing is interesting. The Scourge insignia is never a footnote. But I understand that not all banners carry the same weight of intent.*

> *Wisp annotation: The flag knows nothing of the loyalty it announces. It moves because the wind moves. This does not make it wrong.*

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Banner useful. Morgum like banner. Banner do two things: one, tell everyone where Morgum is; two, can hit thing with banner if thing need hitting. Both important. Together: very important. Peasant say banner is "statement." Morgum agree. Banner say: Peon here. This Peon place. That is full statement.

Morgum have five banner. One old one, from first big work. Is torn now but Morgum keep it. Have feeling about old banner. Second banner is red — Morgum work better with red banner, run faster, carry more, hit harder. Red banner for heavy days. Third banner has gold edge. Morgum not know where gold edge banner come from. Found it near road after big battle. Probably very important banner. Keeping it for important day. Will know important day when it arrive. Four and five are same banner but Morgum make them: both say "Peon" because that what Morgum is and that what this territory is. Very clear. Other peons understand immediately.

Banner don't need neat. Peasant say neat banner. But neat banner is banner that not been working. Torn banner, muddy banner, banner wrapped around axe handle because handle break and banner was closest thing — this is working banner. This is banner that understand what banner is for. Territory don't care if banner neat. Territory just need to know it is territory. A banner that has been many places has earned the looking of it.

Rotation important. Morgum rotate banner by season and by job. Red banner for summer and lifting. Dark banner for tunneling. Gold edge banner Morgum saving. When exactly right important day come, Morgum will know immediately. The gold edge banner is ready. Has been ready long time. Very patient banner.

> *Acolyte annotation: The found banner with the gold edge may be of interest to the Cult. Certain insignia travel in ways their original bearers did not anticipate or intend.*

> *Wisp annotation: The thing named "Peon" on cloth is not what Peon is. But the naming is sincere. Sincerity is its own old magic, older than most flags.*

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

Banner placement within the doctrine of the Cult is not a matter of aesthetics, nor of visibility, nor of what might be termed pride of household, a concept with which I have limited familiarity but which I am told is important to the living. Banner placement is positional doctrine. The position of the insignia determines the nature of the claim being made. This is not metaphor. This is specification.

The primary Scourge insignia is placed at the entrance of any dedicated space at precisely eye height, measured from the ground to the centre of the device. Eye height is not approximate; it is the height at which an individual of standard stature, neither stooping nor straining, looks directly ahead. The insignia should meet that gaze. The insignia should not be looked down upon. The insignia should not require looking up to find. The insignia is level, and the world orients to it accordingly. I have realigned forty-seven banners during a single campaign march and I will not pretend this was spiritually unrewarding.

Secondary insignia — placed at interior walls, chamber entrances, along processional approaches — follow a descending scale. Each secondary placement is two hands' width lower than the last. This communicates, to those who can read the language, the direction of decreasing separation from the consecrated centre as one moves inward, and the corresponding increase in the authority vested in that space and in those responsible for its maintenance and continuation and, in time, expansion. If you have ever entered a Cult facility and felt that the corridors became, as some visitors have described it, "increasingly purposeful," this is the placement doctrine at work. It is entirely deliberate. The arrangement communicates to all parties the precise nature of the space and who holds authority within it and in what gradations and by what means that authority will be maintained and extended into all adjacent territories and populations in whatever state those populations may find themselves by the time the extension is complete. This is standard interior signage doctrine. I am not certain why I am sometimes asked to leave after explaining it.

The fabric of the insignia should be maintained. I inspect every banner on a cycle of twenty-eight days, aligned with the lunar interval, because it is tidier to connect maintenance schedules to celestial cycles than to arbitrary human dates. If the fabric has faded, it is replaced. If the pole has corroded, it is replaced. If the insignia has been moved from its documented position — even by a handspan, even by accident, even by an acolyte who did not understand the significance of exact placement and has since been made to understand it very thoroughly — the insignia is returned to position and the incident is recorded. Everything is recorded. The records are kept. The records will be consulted by whomever requires them, in whatever time that consultation becomes necessary.

## Several Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

A tree that has grown for three hundred years at the edge of a meadow does not require a sign. The meadow already knows. The stones at the base already know. The deer that pass in the morning pass exactly as far from the trunk as they have always passed, not because they have read a marker but because they understand, the way living things understand the edges of other living things, where one presence ends and another begins.

The small creatures make marks on cloth and put the cloth at heights and argue about the heights and take the cloth down and put up different cloth and sometimes fight about whose cloth arrived first. Watching from the canopy, it is recognizable. The forest also had to learn where it was. This took considerably longer and involved no poles, but the impulse is the same: to say, with something external and visible, I am here, this is where here is, here belongs to what I am.

What the cloth cannot do is what the tree does, which is grow the claim into the ground until it is no longer separate from the territory but has become it. The roots do not announce. The roots simply extend, two hundred feet in every direction, below the frost line, in the dark, without ceasing. There is no wind strong enough to take down that banner. There is no neighbour tall enough to fly one higher.

And yet. The creatures with their poles and their chosen colours — there is something in the choosing that matters and that the tree, growing where it grew because a seed fell where it fell, does not have. The tree did not select its meadow. The one who raises the cloth selected a lord, and a colour, and a height, and a morning to climb the ladder. This is smaller than roots. It is also different in a way the forest finds, turning the thought over slowly in the long time it has for turning thoughts, worth noting.

The cloth tears. The pole rusts. The loyalty, if it was real, does not require the cloth to continue. The forest watches the banners rise and fall across the centuries and finds the whole arrangement both understandable and, in the way that very large things regard very small things that are trying very hard, quietly endearing.
