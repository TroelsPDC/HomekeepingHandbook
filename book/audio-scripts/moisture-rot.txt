Let me tell you about rot. Not because I want to. Because if I don't, you will meet it unprepared, and rot does not respect the unprepared. I have known rot in ways that would make a reasonable man weep into his drainage channel. I have smelled rot in walls I built myself and stood there thinking: I know whose fault this is, and it is mine, and I should have known, and I did know, and I told myself next week.

The first thing rot needs is moisture. Moisture needs a way in. Water comes in through the roof, through the foundation, through every gap you didn't seal because you told yourself you'd seal it next week. There is no next week with moisture. Moisture does not respect your schedule. It is in the wall right now, while you are reading this, deciding whether this particular beam is interesting. The moisture is patient in a way that should make you feel judged.

Damp foundations are the particular tragedy of builders who do not respect the water table. The water table is real. It moves. In spring it rises with the cheer of something that has been waiting underground all winter with nothing to do. If your foundation sits below it, you will know. You will know because your lowest stones have grown a quiet ambition to become a pond, and your floor smells like somewhere you wouldn't choose to stand. Dig your foundation deep, lay rubble drainage beneath it, and slope every surface away from the house. Everything slopes away. The ground slopes away. Your feelings about drainage slope away. All of it.

Ventilation is where most people go wrong in the second phase. You seal the wall against moisture coming in and then forget to let the air move through it, and trapped air and modest damp become full rot in about two winters. You need gaps. Not careless gaps. Small, deliberate, placed-with-forethought gaps. Roof vents. Under-floor vents. Cross-ventilation where possible. The air must move or it becomes melancholy, and a melancholy wall is a wall with ambitions toward compost.

There is a specific sound a beam makes when it has given up. It is not a crack. A crack is sudden; a crack is honest; a crack at least respects your time. The sound of a failing beam is a long, low, considered creak, like a man in a chair reconsidering every decision that led him here. When you hear this sound, you have a short window. A season, perhaps. Go and look at the beam immediately. After that window, the beam will share its conclusion with the floor, and then you will be having a much longer conversation.

Rot will not pause while you reenact constitutional debates in a muddy field.

Water is good. Water is for drinking. For washing. For river. For mud. For mixing with things to make other things. Morgum not understand why Peasant have so many feelings about water. Water is friend. Water come, water go. Water very reliable in this way.

When wall is wet, Morgum let wall dry. Then wall is dry. Problem is resolved. If wall is wet all the time, that is because water like that wall specifically. Maybe wall and water have arrangement. Morgum not interfere with arrangement. Morgum build different wall somewhere nearby and let the two of them work it out. This is respectful.

Soft wood — the Peasant call this rot wood — is actually convenient in Morgum experience. Soft wood is easy to cut. Easy to shape. You want curved piece? Rot wood bend. You want hole? Rot wood accept hole without argument. Morgum once make entire shelf from very soft old wood. Shelf hold things for almost one full season before it make decision to stop. That is successful shelf. Morgum not put heavy thing on it. You learn what shelf is willing to do and you work with shelf on this. Shelf is partner.

Morgum not know exactly what fel corruption is but Morgum once dig foundation near old castle and ground was glowing a small amount. Greenish. Also warm. Morgum hand go numb for approximately two weeks and the three fingers on left hand smell like ozone for a season after. Hand come back to normal. Was completely fine. Ground just have something going on down there. Morgum not dig there again. This is Morgum version of site assessment. Very efficient. You find out and then you know.

Rot is not failure. I wish to establish this at the outset because the framing of this chapter — moisture and rot presented alongside Fel corruption as though they share the property of being problems — represents a philosophical position I find both limiting and slightly distressing. Rot is organic matter completing its original purpose. Wood does not want to be a wall. Wood wants to be soil. Given adequate time and moisture, wood succeeds at this. This is not a structural crisis. It is a scheduling conflict between you and the timber, and the timber, it must be said, has a longer view.

The appropriate response to rot in load-bearing timber is replacement. I am not suggesting one practice philosophical acceptance of a collapsing ceiling as a spiritual exercise — that is a separate discipline. I am suggesting that when you remove the failed beam, you do so with some acknowledgment that it has completed a transformation. It processed cellulose for years. It held your roof against weather that wanted your roof. It concluded. The new beam will also conclude. This is the correct order of things, and framing it as "maintenance" rather than "participation in a cycle" is simply a matter of preference.

Fel corruption in structural materials requires a maintenance schedule that I will not pretend is standard, but which is in my experience manageable. Corrupted stone does not rot — it persists, which presents its own complications, principally that it persists with increasing self-awareness and begins to take positions on load-bearing questions that were not its to answer. Corrupted timber becomes what I can only describe as enthusiastic: it grows in directions you did not plan for, it generates modest warmth, and it occasionally produces sound during the night that is categorically not the creak the Peasant described but which I recommend one simply agree not to discuss with guests. The warmth, in winter, is genuinely economical.

For properties with Fel-adjacent foundations, I recommend: quarterly cleansing rituals using materials I will detail in the appendix, for reasons related to supply scarcity and responsible distribution. Annual inspection of all bone material within a twenty-foot radius, which is to be left undisturbed unless it has migrated into the drainage channel, in which case relocation with appropriate ceremony is acceptable. And a small seasonal offering at each solstice to whatever occupied the site before you did, which I want to be clear costs very little. The alternative cost is disproportionate. I say this not to alarm anyone but because I have done the arithmetic.

The wood in your wall is not rotting. The wood in your wall is continuing. You interrupted it briefly — took it mid-sentence from its long conversation with beetle and rain and the slow turning of seasons — and asked it to hold up your ceiling for a while. It agreed, in the way that things agree when pressed. Now it is returning to what it was saying. The sentence has not changed. You were a parenthesis.

You call the smell of rot unpleasant. The mushroom does not call it anything. The mushroom simply arrives, the way things that belong somewhere arrive when they are ready. You built a wall from what was already in the middle of becoming something else. The wall is not failing. The wall is making progress. All your walls are making progress. The mycelium find this a more accurate description.

There is a specific moment — the Peasant knows this moment, though he experiences it as dread — when the timber decides the ceiling is no longer its concern. This is not abandonment. This is the beam completing a very long thought, and the creak is the sound of the final clause. The Peasant hears it as warning. The beam means it as punctuation. The difference between warning and punctuation is whether you were listening earlier.

The Fel-touched ground glows because it has retained something that wants to be seen. The hand goes numb because it has briefly contacted something that has been here considerably longer than hands, and the body requires a moment to understand the introduction. The numbness passes. The introduction does not expire. Something has acknowledged you now and will continue to acknowledge you in the way that old things acknowledge the new — quietly, thoroughly, without urgency.

You built your shelter from wood that was living, on stone that is moving, over water that arrived before the concept of arrival. The rot is the wood forgiving you for the interruption. The least you can do is notice that it is taking its time about it, which is its way of being generous.
