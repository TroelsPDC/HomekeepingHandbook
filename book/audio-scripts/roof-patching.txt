A roof has one duty: stay between you and the rain. If it fails, nothing else matters. I have spent thirty years proving this while ankle-deep in other people's ceiling water.

Pick materials honestly. Thatch is cheap and warm, but it rots faster than promises at tax time. Shingle is practical and easy to replace one piece at a time. Slate is best if your rafters can carry it and your purse can survive it.

Find leaks from the top, not from the drip mark over your bed. Water travels along rafters before it falls. Check in dry weather for daylight, cracks, and soft underlayer, then cut back to sound material before patching.

Patch only when dry. Two dry days minimum; longer for thatch or mortar. Wet patching is how you create a second leak while trying to fix the first. If you catch yourself debating roof theory like scholars arguing about swallows, get off the ladder and seal the hole.

Bring chalk, spare nails, and one bucket of plain sense. Mark every suspect spot before you start pulling material, then patch from highest damage downward so runoff stops helping the leak. A modest patch done methodically beats heroic improvisation done in panic.

If roof counsel demands bridge-keeper questions, answer with tar and nails.

Stone roofs are ideal because they accept time instead of pretending to defeat it. They do not rot, and they rarely surprise you in the night.

If you keep thatch, shingle, or tile, remember this: those materials are always dying politely. Inspect on dry days, mark beyond the visible damage, and patch wider than the wound. Half repairs are invitations.

For stone: inspect after storms, clear channels before winter, repoint early cracks immediately. Neglect arrives gradually, then all at once, usually while you are asleep and newly optimistic.

If rain through the ceiling begins to sound like whispering names, repair at once. If it whispers your own name, repair faster.

Rain does not attack roofs. Rain continues.

The leak is the house admitting it is still connected to sky.
