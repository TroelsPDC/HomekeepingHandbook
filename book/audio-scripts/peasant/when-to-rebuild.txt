There are two kinds of men in this trade: those who will rebuild anything given sufficient reason, and those who will repair anything given insufficient reason. Neither is correct. I am neither of them. I have spent thirty years developing a position between those two, and the position is this: you rebuild when the cost of not rebuilding has exceeded the cost of rebuilding, and not before, but also not after. The difficulty is knowing when you have crossed that line. The difficulty is that the line is invisible until you are standing on the other side of it.

The calculation is not only material. A building that has lost its roof and three walls is cheaper to rebuild than to repair, yes, but that is not the hard case. The hard case is the building that has lost seventeen percent of its structural capacity across twelve separate compromises that occurred over eleven years and were each, individually, borderline. The building is still standing. The building has always been still standing. And yet. You look at it on a grey morning and something in you — the part that has been looking at buildings for thirty years — says: this is done. This one is done. We are done here. And you have to be willing to trust that, because it is not sentiment, it is experience that has learned to speak without words.

When you are assessing a structure, work from the foundation up. A compromised foundation means rebuild, full stop, no discussion, no patch that lasts, no repair that is not merely delaying the same conversation. Foundation failure is a final answer. From there: the primary load-bearing members — the main posts, the wall plates, the principal rafters. If these are compromised across more than roughly a third of their structure through rot, insect damage, or fire, you are repairing a building that has decided to stop. You can delay this. You cannot prevent it. Delaying it is expensive and time-consuming and makes you feel useful right up until the moment it does what it was always going to do.

Rebuilding is not defeat. I want to say that clearly because I have met men who treat a complete rebuild as an admission of failure, as though a building that required rebuilding is a building that judged them. It is not. Sometimes a building is simply old, or badly made, or was built by someone who was working with what they had and what they had was not enough and they knew it at the time and built anyway, which I understand, I do not judge that, but the building is the result and the result is what you are looking at now. When you do rebuild: do it properly. Use better material than the original where better material is available. Do not reproduce the errors. Study why it failed before you decide how to replace it. And do it with some gravity, because a structure has served you, however poorly, and a moment of recognition costs nothing.
