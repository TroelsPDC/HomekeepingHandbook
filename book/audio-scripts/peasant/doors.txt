Yes. Obviously yes. I want this established immediately and without qualification, without caveat, without the sort of performative open-mindedness that allows a question like this to exist in a book that is ostensibly about home maintenance. Doors are necessary. They are necessary in the way that roofs are necessary and floors are necessary — not as amenities but as the foundational decisions that separate a building from a pile of walls arranged near each other in a suggestive configuration. I am genuinely troubled that this is a chapter.

Let us begin with hinges, because hinges are where most people reveal their true character. An exterior door requires three hinges. Not two. I am aware that two hinges will technically hold a door. I am also aware that "technically holding" and "properly holding" are two different conditions separated by time and weather and the particular enthusiasm with which people come through doors after an argument. Three hinges distribute the load correctly across the full height of the door, reduce stress on the frame at the topmost point, and ensure that when the door is opened with force — which it will be, because people are like that — the frame responds with something other than a long slow groan of structural disappointment. The hinges themselves should be iron, or quality steel. Anything else is hope wearing the costume of hardware.

The threshold deserves particular attention and receives almost none. The gap between the bottom of a closed door and the floor should admit nothing thicker than a thin coin laid flat against the stone — that is my measurement and I will stand behind it in a court of law. Anything more than this and you are not sealing a building; you are suggesting one. Cold air enters through gaps. Cold air, moving low along the floor, makes a room feel permanently wrong — not cold exactly, but *restless*, as though the house itself is uncertain about its choices. Fit a proper draught strip along the door's bottom edge. Replace it annually, because it will compress flat with use, and a compressed draught strip is not a draught strip; it is a memory of a draught strip, and memories do not stop wind.

The fit of the door in its frame is a precise thing, not an approximate one. A door should move smoothly through its full arc with perhaps two coin-thicknesses of clearance on the latch side and rather less on the hinge side. I have seen doors installed with visible daylight along their length in the middle of the afternoon — not a hairline, not a suggestion of daylight, but confirmed, actual, noon-strength daylight passing freely through the side of someone's front door as though invited. The carpenter responsible for that door has not apologized to me. He knows what he did. Planing the door to achieve a correct fit is not optional finishing work. It is the work. It is what you were hired to do. Everything before it was preparation.

I have hung forty-seven doors in my career as of this writing. Of those, perhaps thirty-two fit correctly on the first installation. The other fifteen I think about. Not often. But sometimes in the quiet hours I think about the door in Hillsbrad that closed but did not quite latch, and the one in Dalaran that opened toward the wall when the frame was clearly marked *pull*, and the beautiful ash door in Stratholme — before Stratholme, obviously — that was hung on hinges so poorly cast they wore down to nothing inside a single season. I think about these doors. They are part of me now. They follow me.
