The selection of load-bearing timber is not, I wish to make clear from the very beginning, a matter of preference. It is not the sort of thing where a man may shrug and say "well, I had some pine about." It is a matter of structural integrity, of physics, of the basic dignity owed to a roof that is asked to remain *above* you rather than *become around* you. I have strong feelings on this subject because I have stood in the rubble of buildings where someone made the wrong choice, and those buildings made their feelings known in a dramatic and expensive fashion, typically on a Tuesday, typically in the rain.

Let us begin with species. Oak is the correct answer for primary load-bearing members. English oak, ideally; sessile oak if you're being reasonable; anything labeled simply "oak" from a reputable supplier if you're pressed for options. It is dense, it is strong along the grain, and it possesses what I can only describe as *structural conviction* — a quality you will not find in pine, no matter how large the pine in question. Ash works well for secondary members. Elm, if you can source it, has excellent resistance to splitting under shear. What you must not use, under any circumstances, is green wood. I must be very clear about this. Green wood is wood that has not been properly seasoned. Green wood still contains moisture — sometimes as much as fifty percent by weight. Green wood will dry, and as it dries, it will warp, check, and twist, doing all of this while ostensibly bearing your ceiling. I once helped repair a hall where the builder had used green oak rafters. Six months later, those rafters looked like they were attempting to escape. They were, in a sense. They were very successful.

Seasoning time is two years minimum for timber intended for structural use. I will hear no arguments about this. "But I need the building finished by spring" — yes, well, you should have started thinking about timber three years ago, shouldn't you. Proper seasoning requires the wood to be stacked with spacers between each piece to allow air circulation, kept under cover but not enclosed, and left alone. Not visited. Not touched. Not rushed. Left alone to become what it needs to become. This is patience, and patience is the only virtue that structural carpentry actually rewards.

Grain direction matters and I will not apologize for how much I care about this. Quarter-sawn timber — where the growth rings run roughly perpendicular to the face — is far more stable than flat-sawn timber. It is more expensive. It is worth it. Flat-sawn timber will cup. Quarter-sawn will not. If you insist on flat-sawn for financial or stubbornness reasons, at minimum install it crown-up in flooring applications so that the natural cupping works against the load rather than with it. This is not complicated. It requires only that you think for a moment before swinging the hammer, which is apparently too much to ask of certain individuals I have worked with who know who they are. Finally: end grain must never be exposed to moisture. End grain is where water enters wood fastest — it wicks up like a wick in candle wax, and rot follows with enthusiasm. Cap your end grain. Seal it. Treat it with linseed oil or pitch. The man who built the granary on the Westfall road did not do this. I know because I was called in four years later to help pull down what remained of it. There was not much.

Any timber plan justified by demands for a shrubbery is not a timber plan.
