A banner is a statement. I want you to understand that before we discuss placement, because placement follows from meaning, and meaning is the thing most men get wrong when they pick up a length of cloth and a pole and start deciding things. Your banner says: I am here, I am of this house, I serve this lord, this is the place we inhabit. It does not say: I am very important. It does not say: Look how high up I have managed to put a thing. And yet. And yet.

The correct placement for a household banner is above the main door, at a height that can be reached with a standard ladder and maintained without the involvement of a second person and a rope. This is not a limitation; this is a specification. A banner at correct height can be cleaned when it is soiled, repaired when it is torn, lowered when the lord commands a sign of mourning, and generally tended to as the statement of loyalty it is meant to be. A banner at incorrect height can do none of these things. A banner at incorrect height is a banner that has been installed in defiance of physics and good sense, and in my experience, a man who installs his banner too high is a man who has confused visibility with significance. He believes the banner should be seen at great distance. It should not, primarily, be seen at great distance. It should primarily be there. There is a difference, and the difference is everything.

I have seen men put banners on the peak of their roofs. I have seen this with my own eyes and I am still not entirely reconciled to it. The peak of the roof is where rain drains away from and where birds stand to look at things. It is not a symbolic location in any political sense I have encountered. A banner on the peak of a roof suggests a man who has run out of wall and refused to accept the conclusion. Furthermore, the structural load of a banner pole sunk into roofing material is not trivial, particularly in wind — I have patched the damage, three separate households, all three put the banner there because their neighbour had put his banner very high and they wished to be higher. This is not how banners work. This is not how roofs work. The roof will lose this argument and you will pay for it in timber.

The banner should be of a size appropriate to the structure. I have a formula, arrived at through observation rather than calculation: if your banner, when seen from the road, causes a passerby to remark on the banner before remarking on the building, it is too large. The building is the statement. The banner is the footnote. A very loyal footnote, I grant you, and I mean no disrespect to the lord's device, but a footnote nonetheless. The cloth should be clean. The colours should be true. The device should be recognizable at distance and correct — if you are not certain of the correct device, consult the lord's household before putting anything up. I say this because I have seen a man fly the wrong banner for eleven years, and the day it was pointed out to him was a very difficult day for everyone present.

The pole should be attached with iron brackets, not wooden pegs. Wood rots. The angle should be slightly outward — no more than fifteen degrees from the wall — so that in still air the banner falls free and is visible, and in wind the angle gives room to move without fouling on the stonework. This is an afternoon's work. Done properly, it should not need revisiting for several years. I am always faintly amazed that it is not more often done correctly the first time.
