Doors are excellent. I want to say this plainly, lest the phrasing of this chapter's title suggest I am ambivalent, or that I am about to make a case against doors. I am not. A door is an instrument of control over a threshold, and control over a threshold is among the most significant things one can exercise in a deliberately constructed space. What crosses the threshold defines the nature of the interior. This is not metaphor. This is, in its way, architecture.

In sanctified spaces — and here I speak from experience accumulated over a period I will not specify precisely — the door serves a function considerably beyond weather exclusion. It is a declaration: *this space has a condition of entry*. What that condition is varies by the nature of the space. Some spaces require invitation, spoken aloud at the threshold. Some require correct utterance in the proper sequence, which is distinct from simply saying something at a door, as visitors periodically discover at inconvenient moments. Some require that the visitor has, in a rather specific biological sense, ceased certain ongoing processes. These conditions are managed through careful threshold design, and the door is the physical expression of the interior's intentions. The Peasant's instincts about the threshold gap are, I will note in the spirit of collegial acknowledgment, ritually sound even when arrived at through purely practical reasoning.

The undead do not use doors in the traditional sense. I offer this not as a criticism of doors, nor as a criticism of the undead, but simply as a complete and accurate observation for the reader's reference. A door that has been incorporated into a space maintained by the Cult remains a door — it occupies the frame, it provides the visual and spatial grammar of a threshold, it continues to open and close correctly if properly maintained, which I do ensure. Its operational relationship with unliving residents is simply different. They pass through when movement is required. They do not linger in doorways, which is, actually, a domestic habit I would recommend to living residents as well, as lingering in doorways is a primary source of interpersonal friction in any household and the undead have resolved it through a combination of changed priorities and no longer having anywhere particular to be.

I would also draw the reader's attention to what is not a door but functions as one. A ward placed across a threshold controls passage without timber or ironwork. A circle of appropriate material, correctly laid and maintained, is a door that cannot be forced and does not require oiling. A name spoken in the right sequence at the right moment is a door of such precision that it admits only the specific individual for whom it was made, which is a degree of access control no carpenter has yet achieved in wood. These are not replacements for a physical door in most domestic contexts — I want to be clear about that — but in a home that takes its sanctification seriously, they are what the physical door aspires to be. The door is a rough draft of the ward.
