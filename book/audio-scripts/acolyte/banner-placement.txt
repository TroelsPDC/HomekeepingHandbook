Banner placement within the doctrine of the Cult is not a matter of aesthetics, nor of visibility, nor of what might be termed pride of household, a concept with which I have limited familiarity but which I am told is important to the living. Banner placement is positional doctrine. The position of the insignia determines the nature of the claim being made. This is not metaphor. This is specification.

The primary Scourge insignia is placed at the entrance of any dedicated space at precisely eye height, measured from the ground to the centre of the device. Eye height is not approximate; it is the height at which an individual of standard stature, neither stooping nor straining, looks directly ahead. The insignia should meet that gaze. The insignia should not be looked down upon. The insignia should not require looking up to find. The insignia is level, and the world orients to it accordingly. I have realigned forty-seven banners during a single campaign march and I will not pretend this was spiritually unrewarding.

Secondary insignia — placed at interior walls, chamber entrances, along processional approaches — follow a descending scale. Each secondary placement is two hands' width lower than the last. This communicates, to those who can read the language, the direction of decreasing separation from the consecrated centre as one moves inward, and the corresponding increase in the authority vested in that space and in those responsible for its maintenance and continuation and, in time, expansion. If you have ever entered a Cult facility and felt that the corridors became, as some visitors have described it, "increasingly purposeful," this is the placement doctrine at work. It is entirely deliberate. The arrangement communicates to all parties the precise nature of the space and who holds authority within it and in what gradations and by what means that authority will be maintained and extended into all adjacent territories and populations in whatever state those populations may find themselves by the time the extension is complete. This is standard interior signage doctrine. I am not certain why I am sometimes asked to leave after explaining it.

The fabric of the insignia should be maintained. I inspect every banner on a cycle of twenty-eight days, aligned with the lunar interval, because it is tidier to connect maintenance schedules to celestial cycles than to arbitrary human dates. If the fabric has faded, it is replaced. If the pole has corroded, it is replaced. If the insignia has been moved from its documented position — even by a handspan, even by accident, even by an acolyte who did not understand the significance of exact placement and has since been made to understand it very thoroughly — the insignia is returned to position and the incident is recorded. Everything is recorded. The records are kept. The records will be consulted by whomever requires them, in whatever time that consultation becomes necessary.
