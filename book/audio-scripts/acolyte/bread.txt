The Cult does not make bread. This should be stated clearly and without apology at the outset. Yeast is a living organism — a fungal colony, in fact, which makes it rather more sympathetic than most — but living organisms do not belong in sanctified ritual spaces, and sanctified ritual spaces are where we do our work. To introduce a fermenting, breathing, rising colony of microorganisms into a crypt is to invite infestation. We tried it once. The results were not catastrophic. They were merely persistent.

However: fermentation and decay share many foundational principles, and I have spent some years studying grain rot in both its natural and accelerated forms. The decay of grain is not a failure. It is grain completing its intended cycle without outside interference. Left to its own purposes, grain will soften, darken, release its moisture upward, and eventually return to the soil. This is admirable. What baking asks of grain is, frankly, more violent: it strips the moisture away, fixes the structure permanently, and calls this an improvement. I understand why the living prefer it. I note merely that grain's natural trajectory is not preserved bread. It is earth.

The Cult has, in the absence of bread-making, developed several grain-adjacent practices that I feel are pertinent to this chapter. The first is the maintenance of grain offerings: dried sheaves, left in specific corners of the crypt, absorb ambient unhappiness and prevent spectral condensation on stone surfaces. This is functionally similar to what I understand bread does for a household's atmosphere — both practices involve grain and intention, and both result in a domestic space that feels, in some difficult-to-articulate way, more settled. The second practice is grain-ash sigil work, which I will not detail here as it is not strictly a cooking chapter, but which I recommend to any reader whose bread has been coming out wrong and who cannot determine the cause. The sigils are not complicated. The intention is the difficult part.

If you insist on making bread — and many of you will, I am aware — I ask only this: when the loaf is finished and the yeast colony has been destroyed by the oven's heat, take a moment. The yeast lived briefly in your dough, transformed it, was consumed by the process, and is now gone. This is a relationship. Acknowledge it. You need not perform the full observance — that takes forty minutes and requires a candle — but a nod is appropriate. The yeast did something real. The least you can do is notice.
