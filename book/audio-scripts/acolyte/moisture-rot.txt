Rot is not failure. I wish to establish this at the outset because the framing of this chapter — moisture and rot presented alongside Fel corruption as though they share the property of being problems — represents a philosophical position I find both limiting and slightly distressing. Rot is organic matter completing its original purpose. Wood does not want to be a wall. Wood wants to be soil. Given adequate time and moisture, wood succeeds at this. This is not a structural crisis. It is a scheduling conflict between you and the timber, and the timber, it must be said, has a longer view.

The appropriate response to rot in load-bearing timber is replacement. I am not suggesting one practice philosophical acceptance of a collapsing ceiling as a spiritual exercise — that is a separate discipline. I am suggesting that when you remove the failed beam, you do so with some acknowledgment that it has completed a transformation. It processed cellulose for years. It held your roof against weather that wanted your roof. It concluded. The new beam will also conclude. This is the correct order of things, and framing it as "maintenance" rather than "participation in a cycle" is simply a matter of preference.

Fel corruption in structural materials requires a maintenance schedule that I will not pretend is standard, but which is in my experience manageable. Corrupted stone does not rot — it persists, which presents its own complications, principally that it persists with increasing self-awareness and begins to take positions on load-bearing questions that were not its to answer. Corrupted timber becomes what I can only describe as enthusiastic: it grows in directions you did not plan for, it generates modest warmth, and it occasionally produces sound during the night that is categorically not the creak the Peasant described but which I recommend one simply agree not to discuss with guests. The warmth, in winter, is genuinely economical.

For properties with Fel-adjacent foundations, I recommend: quarterly cleansing rituals using materials I will detail in the appendix, for reasons related to supply scarcity and responsible distribution. Annual inspection of all bone material within a twenty-foot radius, which is to be left undisturbed unless it has migrated into the drainage channel, in which case relocation with appropriate ceremony is acceptable. And a small seasonal offering at each solstice to whatever occupied the site before you did, which I want to be clear costs very little. The alternative cost is disproportionate. I say this not to alarm anyone but because I have done the arithmetic.
