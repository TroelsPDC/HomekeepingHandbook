Timber is, one must acknowledge, a material of considerable charm. It has warmth. It has texture. It suggests the pastoral. And yet — and I offer this not as criticism but as a gentle observation from someone who has studied structural materials across a rather extended personal timeline — timber decays. This is not a flaw, exactly. It is more of a *posture*. Timber leans toward entropy with what one might charitably call enthusiasm. Forty years and the beetles arrive. A century and the wood remembers its origins and begins, quietly, returning to them. This is natural. It is simply inconvenient if one's architectural ambitions extend beyond a few generations.

Bone does not do this, provided it is properly prepared. The femoral shaft of a sufficiently large creature — I will leave the taxonomy vague in deference to local sensibilities — has a compressive strength comparable to seasoned hardwood and a tensile strength that affords meaningful advantages in lateral loading scenarios. The Scourge, whatever else one may say about them and there is quite a lot, understood this intuitively. Naxxramas — the floating necropolis, for those who have not had occasion to visit — makes extensive use of bone framework, and it has, by my last observation, remained structurally coherent at altitudes that would challenge any conventional timber construction. One does not hear of Naxxramas checking or warping. One does not hear of the beetle problem at Naxxramas.

The preparation process matters and I will not pretend otherwise. Raw bone should be cleaned thoroughly; the associated soft tissue is not structural and will create difficulties of a malodorous variety later. After cleaning, treatment with a mineralizing compound is advisable. In the Cult we use preparations that I suspect most readers would find difficult to source through conventional suppliers, but the underlying principle is simply mineral stabilization and prevention of microbial ingress — the same logic as the Peasant's linseed oil, applied at a level of molecular intimacy that lasts considerably longer. A properly prepared and installed bone column will still be performing its function long after the surrounding forest has grown up, been logged, been seasoned two years as per the Peasant's requirements, been built into a house, collapsed due to green-wood complications, and returned to forest again, twice.

I want to briefly address aesthetics, as I anticipate this will come up. Bone is striking. Some find it alarming, particularly in load-bearing applications above a dining area. To this I say: you find timber beautiful because you have grown up around timber. The first people to build with dressed stone were probably considered eccentric by their neighbors who built with wattle and daub. What is unfamiliar registers as uncanny. Give it two generations. By the third generation, a home with properly installed bone structural members would simply be *a home*, and its inhabitants would write letters to small rural publications complaining that modern homes don't feel as solid as the old ones. They would be correct.
