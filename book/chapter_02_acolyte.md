# Chapter Two: The Acolyte's Account

---

There is a particular kind of beauty in the act of construction — not despite its futility, but because of it. To raise a tower upon a hill that sits at the threshold of ruin is to assert, however briefly, that something can exist before the dark reclaims it. I have come to find solace in this truth.

The Master's will brought me to this hill at the edge of the living world's ragged border, where the soil smells of turned earth and old iron, and where the distant clash of weapons rings out like a hymn to mortality. I carried my tools. I carry them always. Purpose is the only weight that does not tire the bearer.

There was another with me — one they called Geralt. A human, if the word still applies in these interstitial days of war and undeath. He was a simple creature, honest in the way that only those who do not think too deeply about existence can afford to be. He worked. He breathed. He occasionally offered me half a bread roll. I declined. I do not require sustenance in the traditional sense. But I noted the gesture. Kindness, even in its most unremarkable forms, deserves acknowledgement.

We built in silence, and in that silence I contemplated the tower's purpose — to watch, to warn, to see that which approaches before it arrives. Is that not the aspiration of all consciousness? To stand elevated above the chaos and perceive what the lowly cannot? And yet all towers fall. This, too, is the Master's teaching.

The battle reached toward us like an outstretched hand. I was not afraid. Fear is a luxury of those who believe they have something irreplaceable to lose. I have surrendered such illusions. What remained of me was given freely to the Scourge, and in that surrender, I found I could walk through the noise of war without flinching.

Then the arrow found Geralt.

He sat, slowly, in that particular way that the living do when they have been reminded that they are mortal. The shaft protruded from his shoulder at an angle that was, I noted clinically, unlikely to be immediately fatal. He looked at me with wide eyes — the eyes of a creature confronting the possibility of its own cessation. I know those eyes. I wore them once.

I knelt beside him. I applied what care I could — I am not without knowledge of the body's mechanics, even the living body. I said nothing of comfort, because what comfort is there that is not a lie? But I stayed beside him until his breathing steadied, and then I rose, and I finished the tower.

Because the Master's work does not pause for the suffering of individuals. Because towers must be built. Because to complete a thing is the only defiance available to those who serve.

When they carried him away, I stood at the top of the finished tower and looked out across the killing field below. The sun was setting. The light was the colour of old blood.

It was, I admit, rather beautiful.

*Serve. Build. Endure.*
