# Part IV, Chapter 3: When to Rebuild Completely

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

There are two kinds of men in this trade: those who will rebuild anything given sufficient reason, and those who will repair anything given insufficient reason. Neither is correct. I am neither of them. I have spent thirty years developing a position between those two, and the position is this: you rebuild when the cost of not rebuilding has exceeded the cost of rebuilding, and not before, but also not after. The difficulty is knowing when you have crossed that line. The difficulty is that the line is invisible until you are standing on the other side of it.

The calculation is not only material. A building that has lost its roof and three walls is cheaper to rebuild than to repair, yes, but that is not the hard case. The hard case is the building that has lost seventeen percent of its structural capacity across twelve separate compromises that occurred over eleven years and were each, individually, borderline. The building is still standing. The building has always been still standing. And yet. You look at it on a grey morning and something in you — the part that has been looking at buildings for thirty years — says: this is done. This one is done. We are done here. And you have to be willing to trust that, because it is not sentiment, it is experience that has learned to speak without words.

When you are assessing a structure, work from the foundation up. A compromised foundation means rebuild, full stop, no discussion, no patch that lasts, no repair that is not merely delaying the same conversation. Foundation failure is a final answer. From there: the primary load-bearing members — the main posts, the wall plates, the principal rafters. If these are compromised across more than roughly a third of their structure through rot, insect damage, or fire, you are repairing a building that has decided to stop. You can delay this. You cannot prevent it. Delaying it is expensive and time-consuming and makes you feel useful right up until the moment it does what it was always going to do.

Rebuilding is not defeat. I want to say that clearly because I have met men who treat a complete rebuild as an admission of failure, as though a building that required rebuilding is a building that judged them. It is not. Sometimes a building is simply old, or badly made, or was built by someone who was working with what they had and what they had was not enough and they knew it at the time and built anyway, which I understand, I do not judge that, but the building is the result and the result is what you are looking at now. When you do rebuild: do it properly. Use better material than the original where better material is available. Do not reproduce the errors. Study why it failed before you decide how to replace it. And do it with some gravity, because a structure has served you, however poorly, and a moment of recognition costs nothing.


If this when to rebuild debate turns into African-or-European swallow theory, someone is dodging the actual work.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The question of when to rebuild completely is one the Scourge has considered in some depth, as it applies to structures and to other things. The threshold, from our perspective, is when the soul has sufficient value. I will not elaborate on value to whom, as this is the sort of specification that tends to shift the focus of a conversation unhelpfully. Suffice to say that the evaluation is made, the evaluation has criteria, and the criteria are applied with consistency.

For structures: we use the term *structural memory* to describe the accumulated decisions embedded in a building's frame — the choices of angle and joint and material that define what the building fundamentally is. When this memory becomes ambiguous, repair is misleading. You are not repairing the building; you are overlaying new decisions on compromised old ones, and the result is a structure that is uncertain about itself at depth. This uncertainty tends to express itself at inconvenient moments. Complete reconstruction eliminates the ambiguity. The new structure knows what it is because it was made to know. It does not contain contradictions from three different repair campaigns by three different hands with three different understandings of what the structure was supposed to be.

I will acknowledge, as an act of intellectual honesty, that the question "when to rebuild" as posed by this chapter implies that rebuilding is the exceptional case. From where I work, rebuilding is not exceptional. Rebuilding is simply the later stage of a process that began the moment the first stone was laid. Everything built is being unmade at some rate. Our work is to understand the rate, to influence it where that is useful, and to redirect it where it is not. A structure that has reached the threshold of complete reconstruction has simply arrived at a destination that was always there. We do not mourn this. We prepare for it. We work with what the arrival provides.

Practically: if your repair plan has more line items than your original construction, rebuild. If the repairs cannot be sequenced without first removing a different repair, rebuild. If you have looked at the building for several days running and your primary feeling is one of vague obligation rather than genuine engagement, rebuild. The building knows. It is communicating this through your own reluctance. Listen to the building.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

A tree falls. This is not the end of the tree. The tree becomes something that was a tree: nurse log, shelter, soil, the slow wealth of fungus, the corridor beneath the moss where small things make decisions about direction. The tree does not rebuild. The tree does not stand up again and try to be the tree it was before the wind came. It becomes something else. This is not resignation. This is not failure. This is the tree continuing in a different form, less vertical, more distributed, more interested in the ground than it used to be.

We observe this without recommendation. We are not suggesting that structures which fall should simply be left to become nurse logs, though this would have a certain ecological elegance. We understand that the ones who live inside the structures have opinions about this. We are simply noting what trees do, in case it is useful information, in case it changes the shape of the question.

The question "when to rebuild completely" assumes that the structure wishes to continue being the structure, or that its continuation matters more than its transformation. Sometimes this is true. Sometimes the structure served its purpose and the purpose is complete and what remains is an opportunity for something that is not the structure. We cannot say which situation you are in. We can say that the question is worth asking before you pick up the first stone.

There is another tree, younger, that grew up from the soil that the fallen tree became. The younger tree does not know about the older tree. It does not carry its grief. It is simply a tree, growing in very good soil, reaching upward without irony, without memory of the collapse, without anything except the uncomplicated intention to be a tree for as long as the conditions allow.

Build, or do not build. The soil will be good either way, eventually. We are not troubled. We are watching the light move. We are noting how the light moves differently through a clearing than it moved through the canopy before. This is interesting. This is all of it, interesting.
