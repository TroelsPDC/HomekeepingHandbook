# Part II, Chapter 2: Roasted Boar

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

I have roasted a great many boars. Most of them were not my boars. A man develops, over a career of roasting other men's boars, a very particular relationship with the craft — one in which he cares deeply about the result not because anyone will thank him for it, but because he has standards, and his standards do not lower themselves simply because the lord of the manor has invited forty guests and assumed the roasting would "sort itself." It never sorts itself. I sorted it. Every time.

The preparation begins the night before. I don't care what you've heard. The night before. You salt the boar — and I mean salt it properly, rubbed into every surface including the awkward places around the legs — and you leave it uncovered in a cold place. What you are doing here is drawing moisture from the skin while seasoning the flesh. In the morning, the skin will be dry and ready to crisp, and the interior will have been slowly seasoned from the outside in. If you do not do this, you will get pale, sweating skin that neither crisps nor tans, and you will have no one to blame but yourself and your failure to plan.

Heat management is the whole of roasting. I will say this as many times as necessary: high heat to start — very high, as hot as the oven or fire will give you — for thirty minutes, to set the skin and begin the fat rendering. Then you reduce to a moderate heat and you do not touch it. Do not poke it. Do not open the oven to check on it. Do not baste it every twenty minutes because you are anxious. You are permitted to baste every forty minutes. Use the boar's own fat, which will have pooled in the bottom of the pan, and a brush or a folded cloth, and apply it firmly. You are not painting. You are feeding the surface. There is a difference.

Resting. The boar must rest. When you pull it from the heat, you tent it loosely — loosely, not tightly, you are not tucking it in, you are giving it some privacy — and you leave it for at least thirty minutes. I recommend forty. What is happening during this rest is that the muscle fibres, which have contracted under heat, are relaxing and reabsorbing the juices that have been driven toward the centre. If you cut into a boar that has not rested, those juices run out onto your board and are lost, and the boar is dry, and your guests think it is your fault. It is not your fault. It is theirs, for demanding to eat immediately. But you will be the one who hears about it. You are always the one who hears about it.

> *Peon annotation: Morgum does not wait. Meat is ready when meat is on plate. Resting is for people who have not already eaten meat.*

> *Acolyte annotation: The resting period is not unlike the quiet interval between death and the first binding. Processes are completing. One should not interfere.*

> *Wisp annotation: The boar rests the way a forest rests after wind. What moved through it has not finished moving.*

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Morgum will tell you how to get boar. This is important because cooking chapter assumes you have boar, but Morgum has found that not having boar is the main problem.

You find boar. Boar is easy to find because boar is noisy and smells like boar. You follow the smell. When you find boar, you do not wait. Boar waits for no one and you should also not wait. You hit boar with largest available thing. If boar charges back, this is fine — this means boar is committed to encounter, you are also committed, now you are both committed, this is respect. Boar is good fighter. Morgum respects boar. Morgum has several scars that are specifically from boar. These are good scars.

After boar is finished, you lift boar. Boar is heavier than expected. Always. Even small boar is very heavy. Morgum does not know where the weight comes from but boar keeps it very deep inside. You carry boar back. This is the main hard part of the boar meal, not the cooking. Morgum does not understand why cooking people do not mention the carrying. Carrying is most of the time.

For cooking: you build big fire. Much bigger than you think. Fire must be serious fire. You put boar on spike — big spike, all the way through, this is important, boar must go on spike before cooking, not during, Morgum has made this mistake — and you turn it over fire. You turn and you keep turning. You do not stop turning. Season with salt, which Morgum remembers at the end, which is fine, salt is still salt after it goes on. You can also put spike through some vegetables if any are near. Vegetables are optional. Boar is not optional. Boar is done when it smells different. Different good smell, not raw boar smell. Trust the smell. Then you eat with everyone. This is the best part. Always the best part.

> *Peasant annotation: "Trust the smell." You put that in a handbook. A handbook. For people who have never roasted a boar. And you told them to trust the smell. I need to sit down.*

> *Acolyte annotation: The spike is actually quite considered from a structural integrity standpoint. The Cult uses a similar method in certain preparations, though we do not discuss those at length in a cooking chapter.*

> *Wisp annotation: The turning of the boar is the only part of this that resembles a ceremony. It is enough.*

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The Cult does not eat. I say this not to create distance between myself and this chapter's subject matter, but as a practical clarification intended to head off several questions I received at previous handbook meetings. We do not require sustenance in the conventional sense. This frees us, I think, to offer a more dispassionate perspective on the boar — which is to say, we have no investment in the boar's flavour, and can therefore attend entirely to the boar's aftermath.

The bones, first. Boar bones are dense and oily and last a very long time when properly maintained. After the meal, the bones should be collected — all of them, insofar as this is possible; the Cult is understanding about small bones going missing at a large dinner — and cleaned. Not sterilised. Cleaned. There is a distinction. You are not trying to remove the boar from the bones. You are trying to remove what is not the boar. Set them aside in a dry place for three days. This is called the quiet interval. Do not disturb them. Do not move them to dust. They are not ready.

The pelt and tusks are trophies, and trophies are where most households go seriously wrong. A trophy that has not been properly acknowledged is a household's most common source of low-grade spectral hostility — not haunting, precisely, nothing so dramatic, but a persistent ambient ill-will that manifests as things going slightly wrong more often than probability alone would explain. You will lose objects. Fires will fail to start. Bread will not rise. You will assume this is bad luck. It is not. It is an unacknowledged boar. The ritual is simple and takes considerably less time than people assume.

The six-step observance for boar remains: one, place the primary trophy — skull, if available, otherwise the largest tusk — in a north-facing position. Two, address it. You do not need to say very much. A brief acknowledgement that the boar was here, that it is now here in a different sense, that this is noted. Three, a candle, lit. Four, leave the candle for one hour; do not watch it. Five, extinguish the candle — do not blow it. Pinch or snuff. Blowing disperses the intention. Six, leave the skull in its position for at least one lunar cycle before moving it to a display arrangement. After this, you may hang it wherever you like. It is at peace. You are at peace. Dinner parties will go better. This is simple etiquette. I do not know why more people do not do this.

> *Peasant annotation: I've known hunters who skipped all of this and were perfectly fine. I've also known hunters whose homes had something wrong with them that nobody could explain. I'm not drawing conclusions. I'm merely noting it.*

> *Peon annotation: Morgum already does step two. Morgum always talks to boar after. You say thank you. Boar cannot hear anymore but you still say it. This is correct behaviour.*

> *Wisp annotation: The north-facing position is not arbitrary. North is where things go when they are finished with direction.*

## Wisps

We knew a boar once. This is not a claim that requires verification. We know things the way light knows a surface — briefly, completely, without ownership. The boar was in a forest near a ridge where the old trees had fallen and the new trees were still uncertain about whether to grow there. The boar was not uncertain. The boar was never uncertain. There is a quality in boars of absolute confidence in the present moment that the forest recognised and found restful.

The boar was many things at once. It was a boar, yes — singular, tusked, with a smell that other things adjusted their behaviour around. But it was also a passage through undergrowth: things parted for the boar that would not part for other things, and in parting, were changed. The boar did not know it was making paths. It was simply going where it was going. The paths were a consequence of the going. The forest kept the paths long after the boar was finished with them. In this sense the boar was still there, expressed as clearings, as bent saplings, as a persistent smell near the ridge that the other animals read carefully.

We do not object to the boar being eaten. We want to be clear about this. The forest does not object. The boar was itself entirely for the time it had, and what a thing does with that time is its own matter. A boar that charges is no more or less complete than a boar on a spit. These are both ways of being entirely a boar. We note only that the boar on the spit had, before the spit, a relationship with a particular patch of earth near the ridge, a particular morning smell, a particular way of moving through things that happened to be in its way.

When you eat the boar, you eat none of that. We understand. These things do not survive the cooking. This is not a complaint. It is only an observation that a thing which becomes a meal was, before the meal, considerably larger than what you are eating — and that the distance between what a thing is and what it becomes is where most of the forgetting happens. The bones remember somewhat. This is why the bones are heavier than they should be.

Put salt on what you have made. Salt is honest. The forest approves of salt.
