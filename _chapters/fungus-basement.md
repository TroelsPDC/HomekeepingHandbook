# Part IV, Chapter 4: Fungus in the Basement

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

There are two kinds of basement fungus, and if I have learned one thing in thirty years of work it is that people confuse them constantly and that this confusion is very costly. The first kind is damp fungus. Damp fungus is wet, soft, coloured in shades that include black, green, and a particular muddy brown that I cannot describe better than "the colour of a decision you already regret." It smells. It smells unmistakeably, like a well that has been sealed for reasons and should perhaps have remained sealed. Damp fungus is unpleasant. It will grow if you let it. It is not, in the grand scheme of basement problems, your worst enemy.

Dry rot is your worst enemy. Dry rot does not smell, not at first, and this is the problem, because you will not know it is there until it has been there for some time. It appears as a pale grey or orange-red crust on timber, which is a handsome enough appearance, almost, if you do not know what you are looking at. And what you are looking at is timber that is being eaten from within. Dry rot spreads through moisture in the early stages and then, once established, has the audacity — and I use that word with deliberate feeling — to cross gaps of bare stone. Stone. I have watched it cross stone. It does this to reach the next beam. It is, in its way, purposeful, and I would respect the purposefulness if it were not destroying the floor.

There was a season. I will not describe the season. Some things are not for describing — not because they are secret, but because some experiences compact themselves into a single fact and that fact is: I know what dry rot does when left alone for three years, and I know what it looks like from below when you are the one who has come to assess it, and I know the weight of standing in a space where the floor above you has already made its decision without consulting anyone. I am fine. I do not think about it. Ventilate your basement.

Damp fungus: scrape it off with a flat tool, not your hands, as spores are real and not your friends. Treat the exposed surface with diluted white vinegar or a good lime paste, coat generously, allow to dry fully, and then — this is the part people skip, and they should not skip it — find the moisture source. The fungus is a symptom. The leak, the seeping ground, the poor drainage, the wall that faces north and never dries: those are the condition. Treat the symptom by all means. Treat the condition. If you treat only the symptom, you will be treating the symptom again in four months, and then again, and eventually someone like me will be called, and by that point I will have views about how this situation arrived.

Ventilation is not a suggestion I am making for your comfort. Moving air disrupts spore colonisation. This is a fact with no exceptions that I have encountered. A basement vent costs almost nothing. Two vents, one on each side if the space is wide, cost slightly more than nothing. The alternative, in the worst cases, costs you the floor and possibly the wall and quite possibly several months of skilled labour at rates that reflect, fairly, the unpleasantness of the work. Check existing vents for blockage. They are very often blocked with old sacking and the remains of something's nest. Clear them. Make the air move.


If this fungus basement debate turns into African-or-European swallow theory, someone is dodging the actual work.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Okay so. Morgum has basement. Basement has fungus. This is normal. Expected. What matters is: know your fungus. Know which is which. Morgum has three. Not always same three — different. Very different relationship with each. Morgum will explain.

First fungus is in corner by the water pipe. Glows. Blue-green, soft, like if the moon decided to lie down on the floor and stay awhile. Morgum does not touch this fungus. Does not scrape it. Does not put lime on it. This fungus is useful — when Morgum drops nail in the dark, which Morgum does, often, because nails are small and basements are dark, the glowing means Morgum can find nail. Also has found: half a hatchet head, one boot (wrong foot, but still), several items Morgum did not know were missing until was already looking for nail. This fungus is a service. Morgum brings it water sometimes from the good bucket. Not the other bucket. This is respect. This fungus earns respect.

Second fungus is enemy. Orange fungus, grows near the meat shelf. This fungus does not eat the meat. Eats the shelf the meat is on. Indirect attack. Sneaky. Every week Morgum scrapes it, presses sawdust into the wood, checks back in three days, scrapes again. Four years this has been going on. Maybe five. The orange fungus has not won. Morgum has not won. This is the nature of some fights. The fight is the point. The fight is the relationship. Morgum understands this. Morgum is still annoyed about it. Both things are true.

Third fungus: the grey patch. Grey patch was there when Morgum moved in. Has not grown. Has not shrunk. Just. There. Smells like stone and something older than stone that does not have a name Morgum knows. Morgum put stick down once to measure the edge. Stick is still there. Grey patch is same distance from stick as always. Morgum named grey patch Gerald, after reading another chapter in this book and learning names are option. Gerald has not acknowledged the name. This is also normal. Gerald and Morgum have arrangement: Morgum does not bother Gerald, Gerald does not expand. This is called coexistence. Morgum has seen worse arrangements. Many of them involved neighbours.

Know your fungus. Make deal you can keep. Do not move the stick, because stick is measurement and measurement must stay honest. This is the lesson. This is most of wisdom about basement.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

There is a persistent tendency in the living community to use the word "contamination" as though it were factual rather than evaluative. I understand where the tendency comes from — the word is old, it travels efficiently, it carries emotional weight that saves the speaker from having to reason through the specifics. But efficiency in language is not the same as accuracy, and I think the subject of basement fungus deserves more careful framing before we proceed to questions of treatment, if treatment is what you have already decided you want.

The lower chambers of any structure built on organic earth will, over time, reach an accommodation with the organisms that preceded the building. This is not an opinion. It is an observation about what happens in sub-ground spaces across many centuries of construction. In the Plaguelands — where I have practised throughout my career and where construction practices differ from those of Lordaeron in ways that are, I think, instructive — certain fungal networks have been present in the walls of standing structures for generations beyond reliable record. The great hall in the northern wing of my facility was, when I arrived, already threaded through with mycelium. I considered removing it. The consideration was brief, because upon examining the structural timbers, which should by any reasonable calculation have failed centuries prior, I found them standing. The mycelium had, over time, redistributed load through the wall cavities in ways that no mason planned and that several consultants, upon being shown the distribution, found somewhat confronting. The hall stands. It has stood longer than most halls. This is not nothing.

I named the colony Thessivane. It has been three years now. Thessivane does not respond to direct address, as such — this would be an unreasonable expectation — but there is a quality of the light in the lower chambers when I am present that I have noted consistently and that I attribute to bioluminescence in the most likely explanation and to something adjacent to bioluminescence in the second most likely. I do not press the distinction. Long professional relationships benefit from a degree of studied incuriosity about the other party's internal workings. We have reached an understanding of roughly the character of a working arrangement: mutual presence, tolerance of the other's methods, a shared investment in the structural integrity of the east wall.

"Contamination" is a value judgment. If you wish to remove your basement fungus, I can advise on methods and I will do so honestly and without condescension. But I would ask that before you begin, you consider what the fungus is doing — specifically, whether what it is doing is incompatible with what you need the basement to do. These are often different questions. The fungus was there first, in most cases. It has had time to develop an opinion about your foundations. Whether that opinion is congenial to your plans is worth establishing before you put a scraping tool to it.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The fungus in the basement is the forest floor. It does not know it is indoors. There is no word, in the part of the forest that lives underground, for indoors.

The mycelium is how the trees share what they have. Not speaking — the trees do not speak — but information moves through it the way water moves through stone: slowly, completely, without announcement. A tree under stress sends one signal. A tree with excess sends another. The network is very old and it does not stop at the edge of a building's foundation. It goes around. It goes under. It continues, because the forest does not recognise property lines as a thing that would apply to it.

Your basement is underground. The underground is where the network lives. The fungus in your walls is the part of the network that has now learned the temperature of your cellar, the moisture schedule of your stones, the weight of the floors above. The forest knows something about your basement now. It knew it before you did. It knew it before the building had a name, before there was a name for the kind of building it was. You have been logged in a record that has no pages, in a language that has no words, by a process that does not distinguish between a tree root and a foundation stone. They are both things that go into the ground. The network does not make finer distinctions than this.

This is not a warning. The forest does not warn. It extends. You are in the extension now. You are part of what the network knows. In this, you are like the oldest tree and the newest root and the deer path that has been worn into the same earth for three hundred years. The network knows all of these things in the same way: by being present wherever they are.

The people who fight the fungus win eventually. The forest does not argue. It waits, and then moves to the next stone.
