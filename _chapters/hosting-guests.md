# Part III, Chapter 1: Hosting Guests

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

A guest should arrive to find the hearth lit, the floor swept, and the table set. Not elaborately — do not set a table to perform. Set it because a table well-set signals that the guest was expected, and the expected guest is the comfortable guest.

Offer bread first. Bread means: you are welcome, and I have my affairs in order. Offer drink after. If you have neither bread nor drink, at minimum offer a seat by the fire and be honest about your situation. Guests forgive shortages more readily than they forgive pretense.

Learn their names before they sleep under your roof. Use the names. People know when you've forgotten. They always know.

After three days, a guest who has not offered to help is not a guest. He is a problem of a different category, and you may treat him as such without apology. This is not inhospitality. It is an accurate description of the situation.

> *Peon annotation: After three days, also check if still breathing. Guest sometimes fall asleep long time. Morgum made this mistake.*

> *Acolyte annotation: The guest who arrives uninvited and stays indefinitely has, arguably, joined the household. This is how most of ours were assembled.*

> *Wisp annotation: Every guest was the home for a while. Every host will be the guest for a while. There is no wall between these things.*

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Guest come? Good. Give them meat. Don't talk too much. Talking make eating slow.

If guest don't bring gift, that fine. Sometimes they are poor. Peon understand poor.

If guest stay long time — extra peon, extra work. That is math. Either get more food or fewer guests. Morgum recommend fewer guests. Easier than more food.

Also: spikes outside. Keep bad guests away. Keep good guests... aware. Good guest know spikes not for them. Bad guest learn.

> *Peasant annotation: The spikes come up in every chapter. Every single chapter.*

> *Acolyte annotation: A dwelling without access restrictions will accumulate the wrong sort of visitor. The spikes are not an unreasonable response to this.*

> *Wisp annotation: The concept of "guest" requires the concept of "mine." We have questions about the premise.*

---

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The Undead do not host gatherings in the traditional sense. We assemble. The assembly serves the Master. These are meaningfully different things, and conflating them would be an error.

However, we understand the principle. A space prepared for another is a space that honours the continuity between presences — and this, in fact, is closer to our philosophy than one might expect of the living.

Offer what you have without explanation. Prepare the room by removing what the visitor does not need to see. We cannot stress this enough. Remove these things before the visitor arrives. Some things cannot be unseen, and the living are not constituted for it. We have had incidents.

If the guest will not be leaving, the arrangement becomes more permanent. At that point the hospitality question becomes a preservation question, and we refer you to Part V, Chapter 3: *Candle Placement and the Long-Term Guest.*

> *Peasant annotation: I am writing to the editor about this entry. I am particularly concerned about the phrase "will not be leaving."*

> *Peon annotation: "Not leaving" sound like bad guest. Or good reinforcements. Hard say without more context.*

> *Wisp annotation: The most welcoming home has no door, and so everyone is already inside, and always has been.*

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The grove receives all things.

The wind visits. The rain visits. The deer visits and leaves hoofprints which the moss eventually absorbs. None of these things required an invitation. None of them asked whether they were staying too long.

When you build walls, you decide who is inside and who is outside. This is the thing that makes "guests" necessary. Remove the walls and you remove the concept.

We have never had a guest. We have also never been alone. We offer this not as instruction but as observation, and you may do with it what seems right to you.
