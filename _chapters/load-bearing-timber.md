# Part I, Chapter 3: Proper Load-Bearing Timber

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

The selection of load-bearing timber is not, I wish to make clear from the very beginning, a matter of preference. It is not the sort of thing where a man may shrug and say "well, I had some pine about." It is a matter of structural integrity, of physics, of the basic dignity owed to a roof that is asked to remain *above* you rather than *become around* you. I have strong feelings on this subject because I have stood in the rubble of buildings where someone made the wrong choice, and those buildings made their feelings known in a dramatic and expensive fashion, typically on a Tuesday, typically in the rain.

Let us begin with species. Oak is the correct answer for primary load-bearing members. English oak, ideally; sessile oak if you're being reasonable; anything labeled simply "oak" from a reputable supplier if you're pressed for options. It is dense, it is strong along the grain, and it possesses what I can only describe as *structural conviction* — a quality you will not find in pine, no matter how large the pine in question. Ash works well for secondary members. Elm, if you can source it, has excellent resistance to splitting under shear. What you must not use, under any circumstances, is green wood. I must be very clear about this. Green wood is wood that has not been properly seasoned. Green wood still contains moisture — sometimes as much as fifty percent by weight. Green wood will dry, and as it dries, it will warp, check, and twist, doing all of this while ostensibly bearing your ceiling. I once helped repair a hall where the builder had used green oak rafters. Six months later, those rafters looked like they were attempting to escape. They were, in a sense. They were very successful.

Seasoning time is two years minimum for timber intended for structural use. I will hear no arguments about this. "But I need the building finished by spring" — yes, well, you should have started thinking about timber three years ago, shouldn't you. Proper seasoning requires the wood to be stacked with spacers between each piece to allow air circulation, kept under cover but not enclosed, and left alone. Not visited. Not touched. Not rushed. Left alone to become what it needs to become. This is patience, and patience is the only virtue that structural carpentry actually rewards.

Grain direction matters and I will not apologize for how much I care about this. Quarter-sawn timber — where the growth rings run roughly perpendicular to the face — is far more stable than flat-sawn timber. It is more expensive. It is worth it. Flat-sawn timber will cup. Quarter-sawn will not. If you insist on flat-sawn for financial or stubbornness reasons, at minimum install it crown-up in flooring applications so that the natural cupping works against the load rather than with it. This is not complicated. It requires only that you think for a moment before swinging the hammer, which is apparently too much to ask of certain individuals I have worked with who know who they are. Finally: end grain must never be exposed to moisture. End grain is where water enters wood fastest — it wicks up like a wick in candle wax, and rot follows with enthusiasm. Cap your end grain. Seal it. Treat it with linseed oil or pitch. The man who built the granary on the Westfall road did not do this. I know because I was called in four years later to help pull down what remained of it. There was not much.


If anyone asks whether this maintenance plan can be carried by an African or European swallow, hand them a ladder and let them prove it.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Wood is strong. Big wood is more strong. This is how Morgum understand load-bearing. The load is there. The bearing is the wood's job. You find wood big enough for the job, you put it where the job is. This is the whole thing. Morgum will explain further but that is the whole thing.

When Morgum built the watchtower on south ridge — the second one, not the first one, the first one fall down — Morgum find big log from forest. Very big. Maybe this big [Morgum indicate width with both arms outstretched, which is not useful information in a written work, but picture very big]. Log not fit in hole Morgum dug. Morgum make hole bigger. Log fit in hole. Tower stand up for almost three season before burning down in unrelated incident. This is not the wood's fault. The wood did everything correct.

The important thing to remember is: if wood break, put more wood. This is not giving up. This is *learning*. The first wood told you something important — not enough wood. The second wood is you listening. Sometimes you must listen three, four times before wood is enough. This is fine. Every carpenter learn what enough wood is by watching what happen when there is not enough wood. Some carpenters learn this once. Morgum is not that kind. Morgum learn thorough.

If someone tell you that you must wait two years for the wood to be ready, ask them: ready for what? The wood was already in a tree before. The tree did not wait. The tree just *was* strong. The tree held the sky and the birds and all the ice of winter without anyone seasoning it first. You cut the tree down and now suddenly it need two years? The tree would hold up the ceiling right now. The tree would hold up the ceiling yesterday. This is what Morgum believe, and Morgum have built four watchtowers in support of this belief. Two of them are still standing.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

Timber is, one must acknowledge, a material of considerable charm. It has warmth. It has texture. It suggests the pastoral. And yet — and I offer this not as criticism but as a gentle observation from someone who has studied structural materials across a rather extended personal timeline — timber decays. This is not a flaw, exactly. It is more of a *posture*. Timber leans toward entropy with what one might charitably call enthusiasm. Forty years and the beetles arrive. A century and the wood remembers its origins and begins, quietly, returning to them. This is natural. It is simply inconvenient if one's architectural ambitions extend beyond a few generations.

Bone does not do this, provided it is properly prepared. The femoral shaft of a sufficiently large creature — I will leave the taxonomy vague in deference to local sensibilities — has a compressive strength comparable to seasoned hardwood and a tensile strength that affords meaningful advantages in lateral loading scenarios. The Scourge, whatever else one may say about them and there is quite a lot, understood this intuitively. Naxxramas — the floating necropolis, for those who have not had occasion to visit — makes extensive use of bone framework, and it has, by my last observation, remained structurally coherent at altitudes that would challenge any conventional timber construction. One does not hear of Naxxramas checking or warping. One does not hear of the beetle problem at Naxxramas.

The preparation process matters and I will not pretend otherwise. Raw bone should be cleaned thoroughly; the associated soft tissue is not structural and will create difficulties of a malodorous variety later. After cleaning, treatment with a mineralizing compound is advisable. In the Cult we use preparations that I suspect most readers would find difficult to source through conventional suppliers, but the underlying principle is simply mineral stabilization and prevention of microbial ingress — the same logic as the Peasant's linseed oil, applied at a level of molecular intimacy that lasts considerably longer. A properly prepared and installed bone column will still be performing its function long after the surrounding forest has grown up, been logged, been seasoned two years as per the Peasant's requirements, been built into a house, collapsed due to green-wood complications, and returned to forest again, twice.

I want to briefly address aesthetics, as I anticipate this will come up. Bone is striking. Some find it alarming, particularly in load-bearing applications above a dining area. To this I say: you find timber beautiful because you have grown up around timber. The first people to build with dressed stone were probably considered eccentric by their neighbors who built with wattle and daub. What is unfamiliar registers as uncanny. Give it two generations. By the third generation, a home with properly installed bone structural members would simply be *a home*, and its inhabitants would write letters to small rural publications complaining that modern homes don't feel as solid as the old ones. They would be correct.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The tree you cut down was already performing the function you now attempt to replicate. This is worth sitting with, not as an argument against cutting, but as a record of what was present before the wanting began. Something was already here. The beam arrived later, with nails.

A log does not forget its shape, only its purpose. The rings inside it are a kind of memory — not of what held roofs, but of what held the rain and the particular wind of its fourteenth year and the ice that came early in the winter it was still deciding whether to continue. You are asking these memories to hold up a ceiling. They do it without complaint. They are accustomed to being asked to hold things.

The concept of grain direction is the closest your kind has come to hearing what wood says about itself. The grain is the tree's handwriting. You have learned to read a little of it: *go with me here*, it says, *not across me, not against me, not here where I was uncertain*. This is enough. You do not need to understand every word. You have learned to follow the sentence.

The Acolyte's bone holds longer. The Peon's many pieces of wood hold sideways. The Peasant's seasoned oak holds correctly. All of these are the same conversation, conducted with different materials, about the same impossible wish: that what you build will outlast the building of it. That the ceiling will still be there in the morning. That the sky will stay where you put it.

The sky has not fallen. You did not need to catch it. But something is held in the gesture of trying, in the beam placed carefully across the span, in the two years of patient waiting, and perhaps that something was worth holding all along. We are not certain. The moon has opinions on this subject but has not shared them with us either. We are all, in the end, waiting for the wood to be ready.
