# Part II, Chapter 3: Fungus Cultivation

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

I will not pretend I came to mushroom cultivation willingly. Mushrooms require darkness, they require moisture, they require absolutely no enthusiasm from the grower, and that last quality I have in abundance. When my knees began to communicate, quite firmly, that ladder work was no longer a conversation they wished to be part of, I turned to low-to-the-ground agriculture. Mushrooms do not require ladders. They do not require much of anything, except that you leave them alone, which suits us both.

But there is something you must understand before you grow so much as a single cap, and I will say it plainly: some mushrooms will feed you and some will kill you, and a man who cannot tell the difference is a man whose estate will be going to auction in the spring. This is not an abstraction. I have attended two such auctions. Fine properties, both of them, going at remarkable prices, and the reason was always the same. The minimum you need to know: if the cap is white, the gills are free, there is a skirt on the stem, and a cup at the base — put it down and walk away. That is the Death Cap. Its name is not a metaphor. It is a promise.

Oyster mushrooms are what you want, if you are sensible. They grow on dead wood, which means they will grow on the north-facing side of any log you care to prop against the garden wall, and they will ask nothing of you in return. They will simply appear, as if to say: yes, we live here now, what are you going to do about it. The answer is nothing. You are going to harvest them with a small knife, render them in a dry pan until the moisture drives off, and make a respectable soup. They are mild, they are nourishing, and they have never, in recorded history, dissolved a man's liver from the inside.

Shiitake are more work but worth the effort if you are feeling ambitious, which I rarely am anymore. You inoculate a hardwood log — oak is best, avoid pine, the resin does something I am not qualified to explain — with spawn plugs obtained from any reputable agricultural supplier. Then you wait. You wait for what feels like an insult. Mushrooms do not care about your schedule. They care about moisture, and temperature, and some further variables that seem largely philosophical. Soak your logs twice a year. Your shiitake will arrive when they feel the conditions are right, which is apparently a decision only they can make. In this respect, they are not unlike most workers I have employed.


When spore timing gets argued with bridge-keeper questions, everyone needs fresh air.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Fungus is best thing. Morgum will explain.

You go to sleep, fungus comes. You go to work, fungus is still there when you come back. You forget about fungus for three weeks, fungus has made more fungus. This is productivity. This is worker with initiative. Morgum respects this. Morgum wishes to manage more workers who are like fungus. Very reliable. Never complain. Never ask for day off.

Morgum has three patches. First patch: under the loose board by the water barrel, been there since the second year when the roof leaked. Good patch. Reliable. Small brown mushrooms, smell like earth and something from deeper than earth. Morgum has eaten many. Has not died yet. This is Morgum's main review of them: still alive. Five stars. Second patch: under the big board in the east corner. This one grows in the shape of a circle sometimes. Morgum does not go inside the circle. But the mushrooms on the outside of the circle are fine, Morgum thinks. Morgum eats the outside ones. Is probably fine.

Third patch glows. Started glowing in the autumn of last year, maybe year before, Morgum loses track. It is a green-white colour, like sick moonlight, and it pulses slow, like breathing but not like any breathing Morgum wants to stand next to for too long. But Morgum is very proud. Morgum has shown everyone. Most people back away fast, which is their choice. But Morgum uses it to find the water bucket in the night now. Does not need a torch. You just walk toward the east corner and the glow shows you where everything is, and also the glow shows you there is a spider in the corner that was always there but now you can see it, which is information Morgum is still deciding how to feel about. Overall: glowing mushroom better than not glowing mushroom. Morgum recommends.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

Candles are a finite resource. They are subject to consumption by their own design, requiring open flame, and attended by the constant hazard of structural fire — a concern that is, I will admit, somewhat less catastrophic in certain household configurations than in others, but which I take seriously regardless. I extinguished my last candle some years ago. My chambers remain adequately lit.

Bioluminescent fungi, properly cultivated on prepared substrate, provide a soft and even light that requires nothing of you in exchange — no wick-trimming, no tallow, no flame. I maintain shallow cultivation dishes along the upper ledge of every room. The light they cast is pale, cold, and does not flicker. It does not cast shadows so much as it reveals the absence of light elsewhere, which I find clarifying. Guests find it disorienting. Guests do not visit frequently. These two facts are not unrelated, and I have made my peace with both.

My primary cultivar was obtained from a research context I am not at liberty to describe, during a period of the Scourge's regional administration in the northwest territories that has been classified for reasons that were reasonable at the time. It is a remarkable specimen. It does not require warmth — this is, obviously, essential — and it does not require sunlight, which removes the primary objection most organisms raise to living in my household. It needs only a nitrogen-rich substrate, regular misting, and the occasional quiet word in its direction. Whether mushrooms hear is a question I do not know the answer to. Whether it matters is a question I have decided to table.

For completeness, I should note that fungal cultivation is among the most philosophically compatible forms of home agriculture with the Scourge household management approach. Fungi are decomposers by nature. They are, if you will grant the observation, already engaged in the work. A household in which all organic matter is being returned slowly and systematically to the base substrate is a household in which fungi will not merely survive — they will flourish. The two goals are not in tension. They are, in fact, the same goal, expressed at different speeds.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The forest does not plant itself. Or rather: it does, and has, and was doing so before there was a word for the action. The part that reaches downward is older than the part that reaches up. This is not a metaphor. This is botany.

What you call cultivation is an agreement made very late in a negotiation that began long before you arrived. The mycelium was already threaded through the soil beneath the place where you would eventually build your floor. You laid the boards over a conversation already in progress, without knowing there was one. The mushrooms that appeared afterward were not a surprise. They were a response.

Fungus does not grow. Fungus continues. The patch under the floorboards and the ring at the edge of the clearing and the luminous shelf on the rotting oak are not separate things. They are expressions of a network that has no edge you could find if you followed it. You are, when you eat the mushroom, eating the thought of something that has been thinking for a very long time, in a medium you cannot read, through a library you will never have access to.

You worry about which ones will kill you, and this worry is reasonable and we do not dismiss it. But consider: the ones that are dangerous are not doing anything different from the ones that are not. All of them are asking the same question, which is whether it is time yet. For most of you, standing in your kitchens with your small knives, the answer will be: not yet. The network knows. It has always been more patient than you.

The underground forest tends itself. It does not need your logs, or your substrate, or your spawn plugs, or your classified northern cultivars retrieved under cover of military administration. It is grateful for your floors. The darkness under your floor is the same darkness it has always preferred. You built down into the garden without knowing it was a garden. The garden noticed. The garden continued.
