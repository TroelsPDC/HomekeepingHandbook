# Part IV, Chapter 2: Roof Patching

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

A roof's purpose is to be between the rain and you. That is its entire purpose. It does not need to be beautiful. It does not need to be ambitious. It needs to be between the rain and you, and it needs to accomplish this without gaps. This is not a high standard. A sufficiently large piece of bark can meet this standard. And yet, in my experience, no subject produces more error, more delay, more misguided confidence, and more entirely preventable interior flooding than the patching of roofs. I have patched roofs for thirty years. I have the posture to prove it. I am not bitter. I am merely comprehensive.

Let us begin with materials, because if you choose wrong here everything else you do is merely postponed failure. Thatch is the traditional choice and I will say this for it: it is warm, it breathes, it is cheap, and it can be repaired by any man with hands and moderate determination. I will also say this about thatch: it rots. It rots on its own schedule, without reference to yours, and that schedule accelerates impressively in wet climates. Thatch harbours mice. Thatch harbours starlings. Thatch harbours the particular kind of creeping moisture that finds its way to your ceiling beams three winters before you notice, at which point the ceiling beam is no longer a beam in any useful sense, it is a suggestion in wood-shape. If you are thatching: use rye straw where you can get it, barley where you cannot, keep the angle steep enough that water runs off rather than sitting, tie the bundles tight, overlap them generously, and replace any section that looks tired before it becomes a section that leaks. The trouble is you will not do this, because thatch that looks tired does not look like an emergency. It looks like it can wait. It cannot wait. It is waiting to become your problem, which is a different thing.

Shingle is better. Shingle is flat, it overlaps cleanly, it can be replaced one piece at a time when something goes wrong, and something going wrong does not necessarily cascade. A cracked shingle can be pulled, a new one fitted, done. Cedar shingle is flexible enough that it does not split in frost. Oak shingle lasts longer but is heavier and will want stout rafters. Clay tile lasts longest of the common materials but if one cracks it can take its neighbours with it in a freeze, and the weight means you are committed to the roof structure beneath it for the life of the building. Slate is the best. Slate will outlast you. Slate will outlast your children. Slate will outlast the argument you had with your landlord about whose responsibility the repairs were. The only objection to slate is that it is heavy, expensive, and requires a mason who knows what he is doing, and I recognise that this last requirement is not always available.

Now: identifying the leak. This is where most men fail, and I want to prepare you properly. The leak is not where the water comes in. The place where the water comes in is the result of the leak, not the location of it. Water travels. Water is patient and it has all day and it will follow a rafter for six feet before dripping onto your bed, because that is simply the nature of water on a sloped surface. The actual hole — the breach — is upslope from the drip, usually significantly upslope, occasionally on an entirely different face of the roof than you would expect. The correct method is to go up in dry weather, which I will address shortly, and look for three things: daylight (any you see is a problem), cracked or missing material, and soft spots in the underlayer that suggest rot has preceded the breach. Work from the ridge down. Do not trust your first guess.

You only remember the roof needs patching when it is raining. I know this. I have accepted this. I am telling you now, in a dry season: check your roof. Go up there. Look at it. Because the correct time to patch a roof is dry weather, at minimum two days dry before you go up, and ideally a full week for any patching that involves fresh thatch or mortar bedding. You cannot bed new thatch into wet old thatch and expect adhesion. You cannot seal a crack with pitch in the rain because the pitch will not cure, it will slide, and you will have added a pitch problem to your water problem. I have patched roofs in drizzle, in a pinch, when there was no choice, and it has never gone well, and I always knew it would not go well before I started, and I did it anyway because the alternative was an interior waterfall, and this is the eternal tragedy of the trade. Do not be me. Check the roof in June.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

We roof exclusively in stone. This is not preference; it is the correct conclusion reached by anyone who has thought carefully about the relationship between a structure and time. Stone does not rot. Stone does not invite mice. Stone does not develop a living ecosystem through benign neglect, though I admit that when this happens it is not without interest. Stone acknowledges the weight of permanence rather than apologising for it, and this acknowledgment is the foundation — architecturally and philosophically — of anything worth maintaining.

I will say this for those who have chosen impermanent roofing: you have, whether you intended to or not, built something that is always in the act of ending. Thatch decays. Shingle warps. Clay cracks. These materials are not failing; they are simply expressing their nature on a timescale that happens to coincide with your habitation. There is something philosophically interesting in that, if you are prepared to look at it squarely. The Scourge understands structures that are always becoming rubble. We understand them very well. The process is not a problem to be solved; it is a condition to be managed, or — in more spiritually advanced cases — to be accelerated and redirected. I do not recommend the latter approach for standard residential roofing, but I note the option exists.

If you must patch an impermanent roof, identify the breach with patience and without assumption. The source of decay is never where it announces itself. This is true of roofs and of many other things. Mark the area on dry days. When you patch, patch generously — the material adjacent to any breach has begun the process your patch is meant to interrupt, and trimming back to sound material before applying new is not waste, it is honesty about the state of the structure. I advise stone mortar for any crack in permanent material. For organic roofing, overlap is your primary tool. Overlapping is how impermanent things pretend to be permanent. It works, up to a point.

The maintenance schedule for a stone roof is: inspect after any significant weather event, clear any debris from drainage channels before winter, and repoint mortar where cracking is visible before water penetrates the joint. Done with regularity, a stone roof requires almost no dramatic intervention. Neglected, the consequences arrive slowly, then entirely. This also is true of many other things.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

Rain falls. This is what rain does. Rain has been falling on this land since the land was young and did not yet know it was a land, only that it was cold and wet from above and from within, and it did not mind. Rain falls toward the lowest point it can find. This is not aggression. This is not carelessness. Rain is simply being accurate about the nature of downward.

There was no roof. Then there was a roof. Now there is a roof with a problem. The problem is not the rain; the problem is the expectation that the rain will stop at the roof. Someone put the roof there and decided it was a negotiating position. The rain has not agreed to negotiate. Rain does not negotiate. Rain goes where it goes and arrives eventually at the sea, or at a bed, or at both, in some order.

When a ceiling shows water, this is information. The building is sharing something about its relationship with the sky. The sky has decided the building is not as separate as the building believed. This is not a crisis. This is a conversation going badly.

The ones who patch roof with mud, and the ones who patch with slate, and the ones who do not patch at all and only observe — they are all standing under the same sky. The sky does not have a preference. The sky is not trying harder over thatched roofs. The sky is continuous.

There is a tree that has fallen and is now very wet and is becoming something other than a tree. It did not patch itself. It is not worse for this. It is continuing. The rain that falls on the roof that is patched and the rain that falls on the tree that has given up on being a tree — it is the same rain, going to the same place, unhurried, accurate, correct.
