# Part I, Chapter 4: Whether Doors Are Necessary

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

Yes. Obviously yes. I want this established immediately and without qualification, without caveat, without the sort of performative open-mindedness that allows a question like this to exist in a book that is ostensibly about home maintenance. Doors are necessary. They are necessary in the way that roofs are necessary and floors are necessary — not as amenities but as the foundational decisions that separate a building from a pile of walls arranged near each other in a suggestive configuration. I am genuinely troubled that this is a chapter.

Let us begin with hinges, because hinges are where most people reveal their true character. An exterior door requires three hinges. Not two. I am aware that two hinges will technically hold a door. I am also aware that "technically holding" and "properly holding" are two different conditions separated by time and weather and the particular enthusiasm with which people come through doors after an argument. Three hinges distribute the load correctly across the full height of the door, reduce stress on the frame at the topmost point, and ensure that when the door is opened with force — which it will be, because people are like that — the frame responds with something other than a long slow groan of structural disappointment. The hinges themselves should be iron, or quality steel. Anything else is hope wearing the costume of hardware.

The threshold deserves particular attention and receives almost none. The gap between the bottom of a closed door and the floor should admit nothing thicker than a thin coin laid flat against the stone — that is my measurement and I will stand behind it in a court of law. Anything more than this and you are not sealing a building; you are suggesting one. Cold air enters through gaps. Cold air, moving low along the floor, makes a room feel permanently wrong — not cold exactly, but *restless*, as though the house itself is uncertain about its choices. Fit a proper draught strip along the door's bottom edge. Replace it annually, because it will compress flat with use, and a compressed draught strip is not a draught strip; it is a memory of a draught strip, and memories do not stop wind.

The fit of the door in its frame is a precise thing, not an approximate one. A door should move smoothly through its full arc with perhaps two coin-thicknesses of clearance on the latch side and rather less on the hinge side. I have seen doors installed with visible daylight along their length in the middle of the afternoon — not a hairline, not a suggestion of daylight, but confirmed, actual, noon-strength daylight passing freely through the side of someone's front door as though invited. The carpenter responsible for that door has not apologized to me. He knows what he did. Planing the door to achieve a correct fit is not optional finishing work. It is the work. It is what you were hired to do. Everything before it was preparation.

I have hung forty-seven doors in my career as of this writing. Of those, perhaps thirty-two fit correctly on the first installation. The other fifteen I think about. Not often. But sometimes in the quiet hours I think about the door in Hillsbrad that closed but did not quite latch, and the one in Dalaran that opened toward the wall when the frame was clearly marked *pull*, and the beautiful ash door in Stratholme — before Stratholme, obviously — that was hung on hinges so poorly cast they wore down to nothing inside a single season. I think about these doors. They are part of me now. They follow me.


If anyone asks whether this maintenance plan can be carried by an African or European swallow, hand them a ladder and let them prove it.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Morgum think about this question many times. Door question is harder than it look. Morgum will say what is good about door, and what is bad about door, and then Morgum will decide. This is how Morgum solve problems. Sometimes.

Good things about door: wind not come in. Rain not come in. Animals not come in — mostly. If animal is clever enough to open the door, then door is not doing very much, but most animals not that clever. Dog cannot open door. Wolf cannot open door. Murloc usually cannot open door, although Morgum have seen a murloc work a door handle for a very long time in a way that was uncomfortable to watch. So: door keep out most things. Also, door tell you where the inside is. This is useful when it is dark or you are tired or you have been away and forgotten which wall has the hole in it for going through. You walk toward door and then you are in the inside. This is a genuine service the door provide.

But here is the problem Morgum have with door. Door must be opened. Every single time. You come home with arms full of rocks — you always have rocks, there are always rocks to be moved — and the door is just there. Standing there. Waiting. Both hands have rocks. Now what? You put down the rocks. You open the door. You pick up the rocks. Then you go in. This happen every day, for your whole life. Morgum have not done the counting exactly but it is a very large number of times to put down rocks. The door asks this of you and does not apologize.

Spikes around the entrance: you do not open spikes. Things that should not come in, they do not come in. Same result as door. But also: things that should come in, cannot come in because of spikes. So is a problem. Similar to locked door but worse because you also hurt yourself trying to solve it. Morgum know this from experience. Morgum have counted. But maybe: door for normal times, spikes for when you wish to be alone and are very serious about it. This is compromise. Morgum is good at compromise when the first thing does not work. Morgum's answer to the door question is: probably door. Maybe door. Door is likely correct. Morgum will continue to think.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

Doors are excellent. I want to say this plainly, lest the phrasing of this chapter's title suggest I am ambivalent, or that I am about to make a case against doors. I am not. A door is an instrument of control over a threshold, and control over a threshold is among the most significant things one can exercise in a deliberately constructed space. What crosses the threshold defines the nature of the interior. This is not metaphor. This is, in its way, architecture.

In sanctified spaces — and here I speak from experience accumulated over a period I will not specify precisely — the door serves a function considerably beyond weather exclusion. It is a declaration: *this space has a condition of entry*. What that condition is varies by the nature of the space. Some spaces require invitation, spoken aloud at the threshold. Some require correct utterance in the proper sequence, which is distinct from simply saying something at a door, as visitors periodically discover at inconvenient moments. Some require that the visitor has, in a rather specific biological sense, ceased certain ongoing processes. These conditions are managed through careful threshold design, and the door is the physical expression of the interior's intentions. The Peasant's instincts about the threshold gap are, I will note in the spirit of collegial acknowledgment, ritually sound even when arrived at through purely practical reasoning.

The undead do not use doors in the traditional sense. I offer this not as a criticism of doors, nor as a criticism of the undead, but simply as a complete and accurate observation for the reader's reference. A door that has been incorporated into a space maintained by the Cult remains a door — it occupies the frame, it provides the visual and spatial grammar of a threshold, it continues to open and close correctly if properly maintained, which I do ensure. Its operational relationship with unliving residents is simply different. They pass through when movement is required. They do not linger in doorways, which is, actually, a domestic habit I would recommend to living residents as well, as lingering in doorways is a primary source of interpersonal friction in any household and the undead have resolved it through a combination of changed priorities and no longer having anywhere particular to be.

I would also draw the reader's attention to what is not a door but functions as one. A ward placed across a threshold controls passage without timber or ironwork. A circle of appropriate material, correctly laid and maintained, is a door that cannot be forced and does not require oiling. A name spoken in the right sequence at the right moment is a door of such precision that it admits only the specific individual for whom it was made, which is a degree of access control no carpenter has yet achieved in wood. These are not replacements for a physical door in most domestic contexts — I want to be clear about that — but in a home that takes its sanctification seriously, they are what the physical door aspires to be. The door is a rough draft of the ward.

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

A door is a wall that has decided to remember what it was before it became a wall. Before it was a wall, it was an opening. Before it was an opening, it was a choice about where the opening should be. Before that, it was simply the place where the outside and the inside had not yet been told they were different things. The door makes that difference possible while keeping a record of the connection. This is a complicated thing to be asked to be.

The opening exists before the wall and persists after it. In this sense the door is not a solution. It is an acknowledgement that the wall's certainty was perhaps excessive. Every wall with a door in it is a wall that has admitted something. Most walls are not comfortable with this. The door is the compromise they agreed to, and you can tell, sometimes, in an old house, that the wall and the door still haven't fully made their peace.

You move through the gap and call it *entering*. The opening does not distinguish between entering and leaving. The opening is not keeping count. The door keeps count — that is part of what a door is for; it is how you know you have arrived somewhere rather than simply continued moving through space — but the gap underneath the door and behind the door and preceding the door is simply the same absence it has always been, unbothered by the direction of travel, available to anything that passes through in either direction with or without a key.

The Peasant is right that the door should fit the frame. The frame should fit the wall. The wall should fit the opening. And then something should walk through all of this and not look back, and arrive somewhere that did not know it was waiting, in a place that exists only because someone once decided to stop the outside from being also the inside, at least for a while, at least in this particular location, at least until the walls come down.

We have never used a door. We are not entirely clear on the concept of *arriving*. But we recognize a threshold when we encounter one — the moment when what you were and what you are about to be occupy the same location briefly, before the door swings shut. We cross many thresholds. We do not mark them. Perhaps marking them is not wrong. Perhaps that is what a door has always been: not a barrier, but a mark.
