# Part IV, Chapter 5: Scourge Contamination

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

This chapter is here because it needs to be here, and because someone with relevant experience ought to write it properly, so that people know what to do. That is the reason. I will ask you not to read more into its presence than that.

The first thing is the smell. Blight has a smell that is not rot and not chemicals and not anything you will find described on a list of things that smell like themselves. It is its own smell. It sits low, in the back of the throat, and there is something in a person — some old animal thing — that recognises immediately what the smell means. If you go into a basement and your body tells you something, believe your body. Turn around. Exit the basement. This is not cowardice. This is the appropriate professional response. Every man who has ever told me I was overreacting about that smell has subsequently agreed with my original position. Usually within the week.

Visually: blight presents early as a discolouration on stone or earth, dark greenish-purple, with an oiliness to it in certain light. It does not look quite like damp, though it may appear in places where damp has been before. Established blight does not feather at the edges the way moisture damage does — blight has clean, distinct margins, which is, once you know it, how you tell them apart. A colony of any age will be slightly raised from the surface and will, in some lights, have a faint shimmer at the outer edge. Do not touch it. Do not use a tool you intend to keep. Do not lean in to examine it more closely. Look from where you are standing, which should be far enough away that you could leave the room quickly if the need arose.

Structural assessment of contaminated foundations is not a task for the untrained. If you see the discolouration on load-bearing stone — a foundation wall, a pier, any course of stone doing something important — you want a qualified mason with specific experience in contaminated materials. In Lordaeron, the clerical district, northern remediation offices. Ask for someone who has worked northern sites. They will know what you mean without further explanation, which is the only advantage of the situation being as common as it has become. If the mortar between courses looks crystalline, or has softened in ways mortar should not soften, do not press it to see how far the softening goes. You have already learned what you needed to learn.

The Light district clergy offer blessed treatment compounds, purpose-made solvents, and in serious cases what they call a ritual seal — a containment procedure rather than a removal, which is, in practice, often the realistic outcome of a serious remediation conversation. I will not tell you I have always succeeded at removal. It would not be honest, and dishonesty in this particular subject causes outcomes that are worse than the honesty. The residue stays in stone for longer than you would like to know. I know this. The chapter has said what it needs to say.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Okay. There is an area. Morgum will be honest about the area.

North extension, behind the old cart storage. This area. Morgum does not go there. Has not gone in two years, maybe three. Before that, went once. Came out. Felt bad for a week — not sick, just bad, like something in the area had looked at Morgum and made a note. Morgum does not like being made note of by things in back rooms. This is a personal preference and also common sense.

So: spikes. Morgum put good spikes around the area, pointing out. Not the sharpest spikes — sharpest spikes are for enemies that move toward you, for things you are in active conflict with. These spikes are different kind. These spikes are statement. They say: Morgum knows this space is different. Morgum acknowledges the difference in the traditional way. The area is seen. The area is contained. Both things are true at same time. Also Morgum leaves things at the edge. Small things. A bit of food. A stone that felt right to leave. You ask why? Hard to say exactly why. But you are living next to something. It is right to acknowledge what you live next to. Even if you do not understand it. Especially if you do not understand it. The things Morgum leaves are gone by morning. Morgum does not ask where they go. Morgum is doing the correct thing and the thing gets done and that is enough information.

Containment means: here, and there, and everyone knows which is which. The area does not get bigger. Morgum checks often. Has tape measure. Checks the same four points on the edge every week. Area stays same size. This is called: things going okay. Morgum says "so far" because being honest is important and because Morgum does not know what changes "so far" into "no longer so far." Watching for this. Spikes are up. Notes are not being made. This is the update.

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

I should disclose, at the outset of this chapter, that I have a professional relationship with the Scourge that may create the appearance of a conflict of interest in my writing of it. I acknowledge this. I am going to write the chapter anyway, because the living need accurate information and because my knowledge of the subject is, I think the reader will find, considerably more detailed than that of most contributors who would be considered objective.

Blight contamination presents consistently, and I find that living people are better served by specificity than by reassurance. The initial presentation occurs at foundation contact points — where earth meets stone, where courses of masonry meet each other, anywhere the sub-structural material is in direct contact with interior building surfaces. The colour progression is: grey-green in the first weeks, deepening through a blue-tinged grey to dark violet across the following months. Advanced cases develop a phosphorescence at the margins — this is not the same phenomenon as bioluminescent fungus, a distinction worth maintaining for practical reasons — that is identifiable to anyone who has seen it once. The edges, at every stage, are clean. Blight edges are always clean. Moisture damage feathers. Mould blooms. Blight has a precision to its boundaries that I find, considered purely formally, quite elegant. I understand this is not the reaction most living people have, and I am not recommending it as a response.

For those occupying partially compromised structures — and there are more such occupants in the northern territories than the northern territories tends to acknowledge publicly — there are working principles for maintaining livable space within a contaminated building. Rooms with southern exposure and adequate cross-ventilation, above the ground floor, are reliably hospitable in my experience. Load-bearing stone above a certain thickness slows lateral propagation considerably. The lower chambers I recommend not using for habitation, and I am deliberately not elaborating on this recommendation. Accept it as offered.

For remediation: blessed treatment compounds are effective for early cases, provided they are applied thoroughly into the mortar joints rather than merely across the face of the stone. For moderate cases, the affected material should be removed — not treated, removed; treated is a story one tells, removed is an action — and reconstruction should draw on uncontaminated quarry sources with no northern exposure in their provenance. For advanced cases, I recommend what I think of as negotiated coexistence: living quarters maintained in the unaffected upper sections, lower chambers acknowledged and not disturbed, and careful ongoing observation of the edges. A stable edge is a manageable situation. A moving edge is a different kind of conversation, and I am available for that consultation. I mean this sincerely.

I have no further comment. That is my further comment.*

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

The blight is alive. This is the first thing about it, and it is the thing that makes the rest complicated. It grows. It spreads. It changes what it touches. These are things living things do.

Where it goes is not where the moss goes, or the lichen, or the green things in the garden. The direction is different. This is the only meaningful distinction between blight and the life you are comfortable with — not the growing, but the direction of the growing. The moss wants the stone to remain stone and to be part of what holds the moss. The blight wants the stone to go somewhere else. These are different intentions. They lead to different places.

There are places in the world where the blight's intentions have been fulfilled across a long enough time. These places are not empty — nothing in the world is empty — but the life in them is going somewhere that the life in other places is not going. You would know the difference quickly upon arriving. Something in the body would know it. The Peasant has described this body-knowing already and he is correct about it.

A river goes where rivers go. The water does not choose the direction; the direction is real anyway. The blight goes where blight goes. This is also direction, also real. The question before you is not whether the blight is alive — it is — but whether you are choosing to be in its current or choosing to stand somewhere else. Most things do not survive choosing by accident.

Life does not require approval to be life. It does not ask. This is not an argument in the blight's favour. It is a fact about what things are. Choose clearly where you stand, and then stand there without ambiguity, because ambiguity is the condition the current requires.
