# Part III, Chapter 2: Ancestor Shrines

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

A shelf. A proper wooden shelf, well-joined at the bracket, none of your rope-and-plank business, fixed to a wall that does not wobble. On it: a loaf of bread, a candle, and whatever object most distinctly belonged to the person you are remembering. This is sufficient. This is what you do. I have done this every midwinter since my mother died and every midwinter since my father died three winters after that, and I have never once felt that the shelf was inadequate.

The bread should be fresh-baked if possible, because the smell of baking bread is the smell of the house where you grew up, and the smell of the house where you grew up is, in my understanding of the matter, the actual substance of what an ancestor shrine is for. It is not magic. It is not ritual. It is memory made temporarily physical so that you have somewhere to stand while you think about someone you miss. The candle burns down by morning. This is correct. The burning-down is part of it.

My father's object is a chisel. He was a stonemason — it is in the blood, apparently, though I did not choose it freely, I should note — and he had a way of working stone that was very particular, a specific angle of attack I have never been able to replicate exactly, and when I look at the chisel on the shelf I can see the angle in my mind and that is, I think, the point. I do not wish to say more about my father than that. He was difficult in ways that men of his generation were permitted to be difficult, and he was also the person who taught me to true a wall, and I have spent thirty years deciding what to do with that, and the shelf is part of how I do it.

Now. I am aware that some of my co-authors have what they would term "more elaborate" approaches to ancestor veneration. I want to say clearly: a shelf is sufficient. A shelf is not a consolation prize for someone who lacks the resources or ambition to do something grander. A shelf is a considered, deliberate choice made by someone who has thought about what memory requires and concluded: not much, actually. The ancestors do not need more than your attention. The bread is symbolic. I know this. I am not confused about this. The shelf is fine.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Ancestors are demanding. Morgum say this not as complaint but as fact, like "sky is heavy" or "digging is how you find out what is underneath." Morgum's ancestors number in the many. Many many. Peons live long enough to have many ancestors, if peons lucky enough to die of something that is not collapsing mine.

Morgum has system. Morgum will explain system.

First corner of hut: great-ancestor Grummish, who dug first tunnel under Orgrimmar before Orgrimmar was Orgrimmar. Grummish get best corner, face east. Offering: two meat, stacked, not touching. Meat touching is disrespectful. Morgum not sure why. This is just how it is.

Second corner: Morgum's mother's mother's mother, who is not known by name but who is known by the way knuckles ache in cold weather. This is how ancestors persist: in the body, not just the shelf. Corner face south. Offering: salt, rough grain, in small pile. Salt for the earth she came from. Rough grain because her life was not smooth grain.

Third corner: ancestors who are uncertain. Morgum not always sure which ancestor is which, especially old ones. This corner is for the general ancestors, the ones whose names went somewhere else. Rock arrangement here: seven rocks, arranged so each rock can see the others. This mean "we see you and we know you are there even if we do not know what to call you." This is important. Name is not same as presence.

Wall near door: no offerings. No ancestors near door. Door is for coming in and going out. Ancestors should not be between you and leaving.

This is infrastructure. Morgum maintain shrine same as maintain floor: regularly, before problem happen.

> *Peasant annotation: The rock arrangement for the unnamed ancestors. I didn't have words for that. He's made it into a system. I am going to look at my shelf and feel that it is fine. I have decided this in advance. The shelf is fine.*

> *Acolyte annotation: Seven rocks, each seeing the others. This is a binding circle of a kind, and it works, which suggests that intent is doing more structural work in necromancy than the literature acknowledges. I will cite this informally.*

> *Wisp annotation: The ground beneath any hut knows whose feet it has held. The offering reaches further than the corner.*

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

I should disclose, in the interest of scholarly transparency, that I do not maintain an ancestor shrine. This is not a matter of spiritual neglect. Three of my ancestors are currently resident in the eastern wing of our facility, and two more are stationed near the reliquary, where their particular talents — one in structural assessment, one in the retrieval of dropped items from inaccessible spaces — are genuinely useful on a daily basis. I see them more often than I see most living members of my immediate acquaintance. The concept of the shrine, as a structure for accessing or honoring those who are no longer present, does not apply to my situation in the way that it might apply to others.

I find ancestor shrines charming. I mean this sincerely, without condescension, though I recognise that assurance will not fully land given what I have just disclosed. There is something genuinely moving about the impulse: you have lost the physical presence of someone, and you respond by creating a small physical space in which their absence can be organized. The bread is not for them. Of course it isn't. But it is not for nothing, either. The act of placing the bread, of lighting the candle, of standing in front of the shelf for a moment longer than efficiency requires — these are rituals of acknowledgment, and rituals of acknowledgment are, in my professional assessment, among the most structurally sound things the living do.

My only practical note — and I offer this gently, as someone who understands that perspective can be unwelcome in matters of sentiment — is that the shrine is a representation of a relationship, and some relationships can be maintained with greater directness. Not always. Not for everyone. The methods of the Cult are not universally accessible, and I would not suggest that animation is appropriate for all deceased relatives, particularly not those with complicated histories or unstable personality structures. But for those who find the shelf insufficient — who want the relationship to continue rather than merely the memory of it — I would encourage them to seek guidance from a qualified practitioner before midwinter rather than after. The queue becomes difficult in the weeks leading up to the solstice.

However, I will note that his observation about the bread not being for them but not being for nothing is the most correct thing he has ever written, and I am going to think about it at the shelf this midwinter against my better judgment.*

## Wisps

We have been trying to understand what is meant by "shrine."

We think it is a place you put something so that something does not get lost. We understand this impulse. We experience it differently. Our ancestors are the oldest trees, and the oldest trees are still here, and we are in them and they are in us, and the word "memory" implies a distance between the one who remembers and the thing remembered — a gap that must be bridged by the act of recollection. We do not have this gap. When we reach toward what was before us, we reach into ourselves. This is not metaphor. It is not better or worse than the shelf. It is a different relationship to time.

We watched one of the stone-workers visit his shelf in the dark, when he thought no one was present. He stood in front of it for a long time without speaking. We think he was constructing something — not with his hands, but with his attention. He was making the person real again, briefly, in the space behind his eyes. This is what the bread is for. Not for the dead. For the living, who need a way to go somewhere without moving.

The Peon's rock arrangement interested us more than we expected. Seven rocks, each seeing the others. He has made a form that does not require a specific name to function. This is similar to what the oldest trees do. The root systems do not know the names of the other roots. They know the presence. This is enough. Perhaps it is more than enough. Perhaps name is something that needs to dissolve before real recognition becomes possible.

Memory is not what you have of the dead. Memory is what the dead have made of you: the angle of the chisel, the ache in the knuckles, the way you know how to stand before a wall before you have built it. The shrine is a place to notice that you are already full of them. You do not need to go anywhere. You are already there.

But the standing, and the candle, and the bread — we think we understand why you do it anyway. The body needs somewhere to put its knowing. That is fair. That is a reasonable need.

We are made of light and the memory of trees, and even we sometimes rest against the bark.
