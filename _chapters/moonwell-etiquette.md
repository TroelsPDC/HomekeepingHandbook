# Part II, Chapter 4: Moonwell Etiquette

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

I do not have a moonwell. I am Human, and I live in a modest stone-built structure in a region where the local magical infrastructure runs more to cursed graveyards than to luminous water features, and I have made my peace with this. But I have visited a moonwell — once, on a commission that took me further into Night Elf territory than I would, in retrospect, have chosen to go — and I have opinions, which I will now share, because someone must.

The first thing to understand about visiting someone else's moonwell is that it is *their* moonwell, and you are a guest, and you should conduct yourself as you would at any fine water feature that is also apparently a sacred axis of cosmic energy and an object of deep cultural significance to your hosts. This means: do not lean over it. I cannot emphasise this enough. The edge is usually low and often damp, you are probably tired from travelling, and the water does something to your eyes when you look into it — there is a depth there that does not correspond to the visible dimensions of the basin, and your brain will send a signal to your body that says *look closer*, and you must refuse this signal. Step back. Look at the trees instead. The trees are also looking at you, but at least they are not bottomless.

Do not drop anything in. Not a coin, not a tool, not a bit of bread, not your hat if the wind takes it. I understand the instinct — it is a large body of reflective water and the human mind has been throwing things into large bodies of reflective water since before we had language for why. Resist it. Nothing that goes into a moonwell comes back in the same condition it went in, and this applies to physical objects, to ambient thoughts, and almost certainly to small children, so keep a hand on any you've brought with you.

Do not, under any circumstances, drink from it on a dare. I say this because there will be a dare. There is always a dare when you are standing near something you shouldn't touch. The water is very clean — I will grant it that; it is the clearest water I have ever seen, and I have seen a lot of water in the course of structural waterproofing work — but "very clean" and "safe to drink" are not the same category when the item in question is enchanted to a degree that makes most human mages go quiet and look at their shoes. Bring a waterskin. Fill the waterskin before you leave. Drink from the waterskin like a person.


If moonwell manners require shouting 'Ni,' you've confused reverence with shrubbery procurement.

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Morgum has been in moonwell. Not on purpose, but Morgum has been in moonwell, and Morgum will tell you what it is like.

It is cold. Then okay. That is the moonwell.

What happened: Morgum was looking at the moonwell. Morgum was looking very close because the water does a thing — it shows you something that is not quite what is there — and Morgum wanted to see better. Then Morgum was in the moonwell. These two things happened very close together in time. The cold was very serious. Then Morgum floated, because Morgum floats. Then Morgum climbed out. The Night Elves were there and they were upset, but Morgum was wet, so Morgum was also upset, and the upsets cancelled out and everyone was just standing there. Morgum apologised. Morgum thinks they accepted it. Hard to tell with Night Elves. They have many feelings but they keep them up in their face and do not share them out.

Morgum's main thought about moonwells: it is fanciest bucket Morgum has ever seen. It holds a lot of water. The water is good quality. It does the light thing on its own, you do not have to light it. This is efficient. This is good design. Morgum would like a moonwell for the work site. Morgum is not sure how to get one. Morgum thinks you probably cannot buy one. Morgum thinks maybe you find one, or maybe one finds you. Morgum is not sure there is a difference.

One question Morgum has: does moonwell have lid? Because the open-top design is good for looking into, and Morgum understands this is probably the point, but things will fall in. Morgum fell in. If there was a lid, Morgum would not have fallen in. Morgum thinks lid would improve moonwell without destroying what is good about moonwell. Morgum would like to submit this suggestion to whoever makes moonwells. Is there a form?

> *Peasant annotation: There is not a form. There is no governing body for moonwell maintenance. This is one of several things about Night Elf infrastructure that keeps me awake at night, professionally speaking.*

> *Acolyte annotation: Morgum's description of the optical distortion — "shows you something that is not quite what is there" — is consistent with the field reports I have read, though those reports were written under very different conditions and by people who subsequently declined to write further reports.*

> *Wisp annotation: The water held him. It holds everything, for a moment, before letting go.*

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

I must be transparent: moonwells have been identified, in three separate Scourge campaign briefings that I have personally reviewed, as a Category Two Offensive Hazard. The light they emit is antithetical to several processes I manage at home. The water is actively inimical to the fundamental principle of the Scourge household, which is that things which have ended should remain in productive relationship with the estate rather than being restored. A moonwell, in a very direct sense, is the opposite of a good host. It heals. It renews. It insists on continuation. These are values I cannot endorse from an operational standpoint.

And yet.

The enchantment work is extraordinary. I say this with full awareness of what I am admitting. Whoever bound that much arcane and natural energy into a stable basin — who achieved thermodynamic equilibrium between perpetual luminescence, hydraulic containment, and magical output without visible infrastructure, without a staff to maintain it, without, as far as anyone has been able to determine, *any* external power source — that person, or those people, or that process, understood something about sustained enchantment that most practitioners do not. I have studied the publicly available texts. I have studied several texts that are not publicly available. I understand the theory. I do not understand how they made it work. This is a rare experience for me, and I find it, against my better interests, something close to admiration.

I would not place a moonwell in a Scourge household. The structural incompatibilities are severe and the counter-productive effects on the local work would be significant. But I can appreciate it from a distance, the way one can appreciate a well-argued position one fundamentally opposes. The craftsmanship is real. The dedication is real. I respect, in my profession, anything that was built to last and has.

One further note, for any acolytes reading this who may be considering the matter empirically: do not touch the water. This is not etiquette advice. This is operational guidance. The effects are reversible but the paperwork is not.

> *Wisp annotation: The water has not changed its mind about what it is. This is why it still works.*

## Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

This chapter is titled Moonwell Etiquette. We are not sure that is the right title. We did not title it. We are not sure etiquette is the right word for what happens at the edge of a moonwell, any more than etiquette is the right word for what happens at the edge of sleep. There are behaviours that are appropriate. But the behaviours are not really the point.

The water in the moonwell is the water that has always been there. It does not arrive. It does not leave. It collects, as water does, in low places, and the lowest place in any forest is the place where remembering pools and becomes still. You look into a moonwell and you see something that is not quite a reflection. What you are seeing is memory — the forest's, the water's, your own, these things are not always separate. The moonwell shows you what the water has been holding. If this is disturbing, it is because you had not thought about what the water was holding.

We are the moonwell, in a sense, or the moonwell is us, or we are both expressions of the same thing that was here before there was a distinction to make between a body of water and the light that lives in it. We do not know how to explain this in a way that maps cleanly onto your language. We have tried. The difficulty is not the language; the difficulty is that the concept does not have edges, and your words require edges to function. Round edges. Square edges. The word "moonwell" has edges. The thing itself does not.

When you stand at the edge of a moonwell and you feel the pull — the sense that you should look closer, lean further, put your hand in — that is not mischief on the part of the water. That is recognition. Something in you remembers being as continuous as water is. Before you had edges. Before you needed a name to know what you were. The moonwell is not trying to take anything from you. It is offering to remind you of something you have forgotten so thoroughly you no longer know you've forgotten it.

This is what the chapter is about. Not leaning. Not dropping things. Not drinking from it on a dare, though we agree you should not do that either. The chapter is about what it means to stand next to something that does not forget, when you have spent your whole life learning how.
