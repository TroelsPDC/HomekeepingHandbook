# Part II, Chapter 1: Bread

---

## Theodore Furlbrow, Master Mason of Lordaeron

Bread is not a recipe. Bread is a covenant. You and the grain have entered into an agreement — you will provide warmth and time, it will provide structure and nourishment — and if you break your end of the bargain by using the wrong flour or not waiting long enough, you deserve whatever flat, dense, spiritually hollow disc you end up with. I have been making bread since before most of you had opinions about bread, and I am here to tell you that your opinions are wrong.

Flour first. You want a strong bread flour, twelve to fourteen percent protein. Not your soft pastry flour, not your all-purpose compromise flour, not whatever's been sitting in the back of the larder since the siege. Strong flour. The gluten network you build is what gives the loaf its spine, and a man without a spine — and a loaf without a spine — is just taking up space in a warm room. Weigh your flour. Do not estimate. Estimation is how you get flatbread when you wanted bread.

Salt and yeast go in on opposite sides of the bowl. This is not superstition. Salt kills yeast if they are introduced directly. I have seen grown men ruin a loaf by tipping everything in at once and then wondering why nothing rose. It rose fine. You killed it. Keep them separated until you begin to work the dough. You will know the dough is ready when it is smooth and springs back when you poke it. If it does not spring back, you have not worked it enough. If it tears when you stretch it, you have worked it too much. The window is narrow. This is why baking has moral weight.

Proving time: once, until doubled; shape; prove again, until nearly doubled. Do not rush the prove. Do not put it somewhere hot to speed things up. You are not in charge of the yeast's timeline. The yeast is in charge. You are the assistant. Accept this. The oven should be as hot as it will go — two hundred and thirty Celsius minimum, with a covered pot if you have one, steam in the oven if you don't. Score the top with a sharp blade before baking or the bread will tear where it wants to rather than where you want it to, and it will be ugly, and that will be your fault.

I want to say something plainly: if you have never made your own bread, you should examine what that says about you. I don't mean to be unkind. I mean it practically. Bread is the most fundamental act of domesticity. Hunting is older, yes, but bread is *civilisation*. Every man, woman, and — I won't be exclusionary — every peon who has ever called a place a home has at some point stood in front of a fire with grain and water and made something. If you haven't done this, you are, with respect, merely *renting* the concept of home without paying full price.

> *Acolyte annotation: The "covenant" framing is interesting. The Cult also enters into agreements with things that will eventually be consumed. We call them contracts.*

> *Wisp annotation: The grain did not agree to be flour. This is worth sitting with.*

## Morgum Soothumb, Senior Peon

Bread is easy. Morgum does not understand why small pink men make complicated face about bread.

You take grain. All grain. Whatever grain. Oat, wheat, barley, rye — Morgum does not separate them, grain does not have feelings about this. Grind it if you have time. If not, leave it. Little crunchy bits in bread are not problem, they are bonus. Texture. You add water until it is like wet dirt, but less dirty. You add salt. Morgum adds salt twice because first time might not take. Then you press it flat with fist and put it on fire until it stops making steam and smells like cooked thing.

Morgum's bread is good bread. It is brown all the way through. It does not go soft or mouldy because there is nothing soft or mouldable left in it. Morgum's bread has lasted three weeks in pack without change. This is good quality. You want bread that waits for you, not bread that falls apart the moment you leave it alone. That is weak bread. Morgum does not respect weak bread.

Some people say bread must rise. Morgum says: rise is for proud bread. Humble bread stays flat and feeds you anyway. Some people say Morgum's bread is too hard. Morgum says: soak it in broth. Problem solved. You were going to eat soup anyway. Now you have soup bread. Everything is better. If tooth breaks on Morgum's bread, that is weak tooth. Tooth should have been stronger. Bread is not apologising.

> *Peasant annotation: Three weeks in a pack. Three weeks. That bread is not bread anymore, Morgum. That is a weapon. That is building material. I will not dignify it with the word bread.*

> *Acolyte annotation: "Nothing soft or mouldable left" — yes. This is the correct approach to preservation. Morgum has intuitively arrived at desiccation. I am somewhat impressed.*

> *Wisp annotation: The bread that does not change has chosen a different kind of future than the bread that is eaten. Both are honest.*

## Vorun the Pallid, Acolyte of the Cult

The Cult does not make bread. This should be stated clearly and without apology at the outset. Yeast is a living organism — a fungal colony, in fact, which makes it rather more sympathetic than most — but living organisms do not belong in sanctified ritual spaces, and sanctified ritual spaces are where we do our work. To introduce a fermenting, breathing, rising colony of microorganisms into a crypt is to invite infestation. We tried it once. The results were not catastrophic. They were merely persistent.

However: fermentation and decay share many foundational principles, and I have spent some years studying grain rot in both its natural and accelerated forms. The decay of grain is not a failure. It is grain completing its intended cycle without outside interference. Left to its own purposes, grain will soften, darken, release its moisture upward, and eventually return to the soil. This is admirable. What baking asks of grain is, frankly, more violent: it strips the moisture away, fixes the structure permanently, and calls this an improvement. I understand why the living prefer it. I note merely that grain's natural trajectory is not preserved bread. It is earth.

The Cult has, in the absence of bread-making, developed several grain-adjacent practices that I feel are pertinent to this chapter. The first is the maintenance of grain offerings: dried sheaves, left in specific corners of the crypt, absorb ambient unhappiness and prevent spectral condensation on stone surfaces. This is functionally similar to what I understand bread does for a household's atmosphere — both practices involve grain and intention, and both result in a domestic space that feels, in some difficult-to-articulate way, more settled. The second practice is grain-ash sigil work, which I will not detail here as it is not strictly a cooking chapter, but which I recommend to any reader whose bread has been coming out wrong and who cannot determine the cause. The sigils are not complicated. The intention is the difficult part.

If you insist on making bread — and many of you will, I am aware — I ask only this: when the loaf is finished and the yeast colony has been destroyed by the oven's heat, take a moment. The yeast lived briefly in your dough, transformed it, was consumed by the process, and is now gone. This is a relationship. Acknowledge it. You need not perform the full observance — that takes forty minutes and requires a candle — but a nod is appropriate. The yeast did something real. The least you can do is notice.

## Several Wisps
### *(translated from ambient moonlight)*

Grain begins as a held breath. The seed waits. It has been waiting since the last field, and before that since the field before that, each grain a copy of an intention that started somewhere no record reaches. The waiting is not impatience. The waiting is the seed being entirely itself, in reserve.

You take the seed and crush it, and in crushing you release everything it was holding back. This is called flour, and it is the seed's potential made powdery and inert and available. You add water, and the flour remembers, briefly, what it was trying to do. The gluten wakes. This is not metaphor. Something in the flour recognises moisture and reaches toward it with long molecular arms, and the dough that results is a field's attempt to reconstitute itself in a new medium. It is working with what it has.

Then you introduce the yeast. The yeast has been doing this longer than you have. The yeast has been fermenting grain since before grain was a crop, since before anything decided to grow in organised rows, since the first wet seed sat next to the first wild microorganism in the first patch of warm mud and they reached an understanding. You are not teaching the yeast about bread. You are joining a conversation that is much older than your oven.

The oven is the only part of this process that is entirely yours. Heat, duration, the shape of the vessel. You impose these, and the bread complies, and what emerges is a loaf: all of the seed's futures collapsed into one dense, cuttable, mortal present. Every field it could have become. Every other bread it might have been. Compressed into this. You put it in your mouth. You are eating a decision.

We do not think less of you for this. We merely think you should know.
